# Retail & Supermarkets Benchmarking

## Core Principle
Grocery and retail apps are used by a broader demographic than most apps — including users who are less digitally confident. Supermarket apps must work for first-time users and habitual users simultaneously. BFM's supermarket benchmarking reveals that the gap between the best and worst experiences is primarily about trust, transparency, and predictability — not feature richness.

## Key Benchmarks (from BFM Supermarkets study)

### Product Discovery & Search
The task: find a specific product, compare sizes/prices, add to basket.

Best practice: search results that handle brand synonyms (e.g., "coke" → Coca-Cola), show price per unit alongside total price, and allow filtering by diet/allergy attributes in one step.

Common failures:
- Search returns irrelevant promoted products before the item searched for
- Price per unit buried or absent — makes comparison between sizes difficult
- "Add to basket" from search results (no product page visit required for known items)

### Checkout & Substitution UX
Substitution policy varies enormously. When an item is unavailable, different supermarkets handle this differently:
- Best: show substitution at time of order, allow pre-approval of specific alternatives
- Worst: substitute at delivery, driver cannot reverse, user must return item later

**BFM observation:** The substitution moment is a trust event. Users who receive an unwanted substitution without being informed in advance lose confidence in the service — even if the substitution is reasonable.

### Delivery Slot Transparency
"Delivery from £X" is the industry pattern — but the full cost (slot fee + delivery fee + surge pricing) often only appears at checkout.

Best practice: show total estimated delivery cost (including slot fee) before the user starts shopping — not at checkout.

### Loyalty & Personalisation
Supermarket loyalty schemes (Tesco Clubcard, Sainsbury's Nectar) influence price — but the price reduction is often shown only for loyalty card holders.

Best practice: Show both prices (with and without loyalty card) on product listings, not just at checkout. This makes the loyalty benefit tangible at the decision point.

## BFM Evidence
> "Personalization signals" — the BFM supermarkets study identifies that recommendations like "because you bought X" are only valuable when X was purchased recently, in the right category, and the recommendation is genuinely related. Generic "based on your shopping" labels without specificity perform no better than "popular items."

> "Trust building through consistency" — price shown in search must match price shown at checkout. Any discrepancy, even small, creates lasting distrust.

## What Good Looks Like
A supermarket app where: the first 3 search results are genuinely the most relevant product (not promoted); price per unit is shown alongside total price; substitution preferences can be set before delivery; delivery slot cost is fully transparent before shopping begins; and loyalty pricing is visible at product level, not just at checkout.

## Red Flags
- [ ] First search results are promoted products, not the item searched for
- [ ] Price per unit absent from product listings
- [ ] Substitution policy not communicated before order is placed
- [ ] Full delivery cost not visible until checkout
- [ ] Loyalty discount only visible at checkout, not at product listing
