# Goal Setting

## Core Principle
A goal the user did not consciously choose is not a goal — it is a default. For a goal to drive retention, the user must feel they own it (Endowment Effect). The closer a user feels to a reward, the more motivated they are (Goal Gradient Effect), so artificial early progress is a legitimate and powerful design tool.

## Diagnostic Questions
- Are users selecting their goal, or merely accepting a pre-selected one?
- Does the app track and surface the user's stated goal at the moment of each relevant action?
- After the user redeems or completes a goal, does the product spin the flywheel back to a new starting point?

## Design Checklist
- Give users enough friction to make a real choice — do not default-select a "recommended" goal level.
- Use the Goal Gradient Effect: give artificial early progress (a free stamp, a head-start point) immediately after goal creation.
- Celebrate real progress toward the stated goal — not platform-level milestones that weren't on the user's radar.
- After redemption or completion, immediately create a new starting point (free bean, bonus progress) to restart the loop.
- Show the stated goal prominently in the product during each session — not buried in a profile tab.

## Anti-Pattern
**What goes wrong:** The product pre-selects a "recommended" goal level, which removes the psychological commitment required for the goal to actually drive behaviour.
**Why it happens:** Teams want to reduce drop-off in the goal-selection step, so they make skipping easy. The result is users who have a goal on paper but no emotional stake in it.
**BFM Example:** Strava — the goal-creation screen shows "Casual / Moderate (Recommended) / Serious" with "Moderate" pre-highlighted. "That, and the fact that the user is being prescribed a goal, reduces the motivation to think about this decision. You don't want to make it easy to blindly accept a default option. If they can't keep to it, they'll associate their failure with Strava."

## BFM Evidence

### Costa — the Goal Gradient Effect, core theory
> "This is known as the Goal Gradient Effect. People are more motivated the closer they feel they are to a reward."

### Costa — artificial progress explained
> "It can actually be more motivating to increase that number to 10 ... and then give people two free stamps right away. Although it's artificial, the feeling of progression is motivating."

### Costa — missing the flywheel restart after redemption
> "But after the reset, Costa doesn't give you a free bean. Which is a missed opportunity. They should be spinning the flywheel. Give them another free bean. Or double points with their next purchase."

### Costa — cashing out early breaks the loop
> "The psychological benefit is the momentum and progress. So why are they promoting you to irreversibly swap your hard-earned stamps for rewards?"

### Strava — prescribed goals reduce motivation
> "The whole point is that this needs to be a realistic commitment. It requires physically going outside and exercising. You don't want to make it easy to blindly accept a default option."

### Strava — completing a real goal goes uncelebrated
> "I completed my weekly goal, and I barely noticed. That information isn't just relatively hidden, there's no celebration at all."

## What Good Looks Like
A fitness app asks "How many runs per week is realistic for you right now?" with no pre-selection and no "recommended" badge. After the user types in a number, it immediately marks the current week as 1/0 complete — but starts a 7-day countdown timer so the goal feels urgent. After the first completed run, the confirmation screen shows progress toward the goal ("1 of 3 runs this week"), not generic achievements. After the user completes the week's goal for the first time, the flywheel restart: "You crushed it — we're giving you a head start: week 2 starts with 1 run already counted."

## Red Flags
- [ ] A "Recommended" badge or pre-selection exists on the goal-level picker.
- [ ] The user's stated goal is not surfaced anywhere on the main home screen or activity feed.
- [ ] After a user redeems or completes a goal, the interface resets to zero with no immediate restart incentive.
- [ ] The product celebrates platform-level achievements (personal records, first activity) but not progress toward the user's own stated goal.
- [ ] Users can "cash out" accumulated progress early in a way that destroys their momentum toward the primary goal.
