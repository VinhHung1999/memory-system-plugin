# Monopoly UX

## Core Principle
Monopoly UX is not just bad design — it is bad design that persists because the company has no market incentive to fix it. When switching costs are high (the tickets you want are only on Ticketmaster), user complaints produce PR apologies, not product improvements. The diagnostic is simple: if the UX is bad and the company is growing, it's probably a monopoly UX problem, not a design capability problem.

## Diagnostic Questions
- Does the company have >50% market share in its category? Is there a meaningful alternative?
- Are user complaints about UX consistent and public, but revenue continues to grow?
- Does the product apologise for UX failures rather than fix them?
- Are fees, friction, or dark patterns worse than competitors in adjacent markets?

## Design Checklist (for non-monopoly products)
- Treat every friction point as a potential competitor entry point — if switching were easy, this would be the feature that drives it
- Never rely on switching costs to paper over UX debt — the moment a better alternative exists, switching cost evaporates
- Measure user NPS as a leading indicator of future market share loss, not just satisfaction

## Anti-Pattern
**What goes wrong:** A company achieves monopoly or near-monopoly status. UX investment decreases because conversion rates are high anyway — users have no alternative. Over time, the product accumulates dark patterns, friction, and fee structures that would be competitive suicide in a healthy market. When a competitor eventually emerges, the incumbent has no practise at designing for user preference.
**Why it happens:** Product teams' success metrics (revenue, conversion) are decoupled from UX quality when monopoly position makes users captive. "Good enough" always wins the internal argument.
**BFM Example (Ticketmaster):** 70% market share, 4 consecutive public apologies for UX failures, growing revenue. The product is bad by design measurement standards — but market share is unaffected because the tickets aren't available anywhere else.

## BFM Evidence

### Ticketmaster — monopoly insulates bad UX
> "Ticketmaster is clearly a monopoly. It accounts for more than 70% of the ticketing and live event market."

> "As is often the case with monopolies, the incentive to improve the user experience is low. And it shows."

### Ticketmaster — public UX criticism with no market effect
> "I don't think I can overstate just how terrible the @Ticketmaster UX is."
> "How can Ticketmaster's UX be so spectacularly poor after so many years? It's a horrible experience that I hate each and every time."
> "holy hell ticketmaster's UX is so bad"

These are public tweets — not user research. The company sees them. Revenue: $3.2B (2006) → $16.68B (2022). No corrective action taken.

### Ticketmaster — fee stacking as monopoly behaviour
> "ARTIST PRESALE: $33.50 x 2 = $67.00 | Service Fee: $15.20 x 2 = $30.40 | Facility Charge: $7.00 x 2 = $14.00"

> "Charging large fees in addition to the ticket prices." [listed as one of three things Ticketmaster is "infamous for"]

Fee stacking this aggressive would cause user abandonment in a competitive market. In a monopoly, the user pays because the alternative is not attending the event.

### Ticketmaster — the PR response pattern
> "Ticketmaster apologises for Taylor Swift / Ticketmaster apologises for Bad Bunny / Ticketmaster Apologizes to Billie Eilish / Ticketmaster Apologizes After Bruce Springsteen"

Apology → no change → next incident → apology. The apology is the retention strategy, not UX improvement.

## What Good Looks Like
A company that designs as if a better competitor is launching tomorrow. Every piece of friction is treated as a vulnerability, not an acceptable tax on captive users. Fee structures are transparent from the first page, not revealed at checkout. User trust is treated as the most fragile asset.

## Red Flags
- [ ] Multiple public apologies for the same class of UX failure, with no structural change
- [ ] Fees revealed in the final checkout step that were not visible in the product listing
- [ ] User complaints are responded to by PR, not by product teams
- [ ] UX investment declining as market share grows
