# Core Value Focus

## Core Principle
The most common product mistake when facing churn is adding features. But churn is rarely caused by missing features — it's caused by users not consistently experiencing the core value. Adding more things to do creates more surface area for confusion, not more reasons to stay. The product that does one thing brilliantly retains better than the product that does ten things adequately. Reduction is a retention strategy.

## Diagnostic Questions
- What is the single action that, when a user does it, predicts retention? (The "one thing")
- Do churned users differ from retained users in whether they did the "one thing" — not in which features they used?
- Is your roadmap adding features to address churn, or deepening the core loop?
- What would happen to retention if you removed everything except the core flow?

## Design Checklist
- Identify the single activity most correlated with long-term retention (not signup completeness — actual behaviour)
- Design onboarding, notifications, and re-engagement emails to drive users toward that one action
- When users are inactive, the re-engagement ask is to do the one thing — not to "explore what's new"
- Measure "did the user do the one thing this week?" as your primary retention metric, ahead of DAU
- Before adding a feature to combat churn, check: does this deepen the core loop, or create a competing loop?

## Anti-Pattern
**What goes wrong:** Faced with churn data, a product team adds a social feature, a gamification layer, and a new content type. Each adds complexity. Retained users now have to navigate a more cluttered product to do the thing they actually came for. New users face a more complex product during onboarding. Churn goes up.
**Why it happens:** "What's missing?" is easier to answer in a meeting than "what should we stop doing?" Feature additions feel like progress; they produce roadmap slides and ship announcements. Removing complexity produces neither.
**BFM Example (Trello):** The "How to Reduce Churn by Doing Your One Thing" study makes this explicit — Trello's retention breakthrough came from identifying the single action that correlated with long-term retention and orienting the entire product experience around driving users to that action consistently. Complexity was a churn driver, not a retention driver.

## BFM Evidence

### Trello — churn reduced by identifying and focusing the "one thing"
> "How to Reduce Churn by Doing Your One Thing"

The case study title is the finding: Trello's churn reduction came from identifying the specific action (creating and using a board in a particular way) that predicted retention, and building the product experience around driving that action.

### Trello — complexity vs. retention is a real trade-off
> "Complexity vs. Retention" (trade-off visualization in the case study)

The insight: more features create more ways to use the product, but also more ways to get confused, fail to find value, and churn.

### Trello — cohort analysis revealed the "one thing"
> "Cohort Comparison: Churned Users" — re-engagement analysis

Churned users looked different from retained users not in their demographics or plan, but in whether they had done the core action. This is the diagnostic: churn is a behaviour gap, not a feature gap.

### Strava — goal-setting as the core retention anchor
> "Strava is a very goal-driven experience."

For Strava, the "one thing" is goal-setting. A user with an active goal has a reason to open the app every training session. A user without a goal is a subscriber paying for a service they don't actively use. The entire subscription value proposition — "Better habits. Smarter training. Steady progress." — is meaningless unless the user has a goal the product is helping them achieve.

### Audible — feature complexity in purchase flow creates abandonment
> "This is the decision that even highly motivated users need to make. So you want this to feel comfortable. It will directly impact conversion rates."

The Audible case study shows the inverse of core value focus: at the moment of purchase (the core transaction), users must navigate three competing payment options, hidden pricing, and multiple modals. The complexity of the purchase flow is a retention and conversion failure — at the most critical moment in the product, the experience is most complex.

> "In my experience, these suggestions are likely to convert better than a generic 'popular' category."

The prescription: personalised recommendations (connected to the user's listening history) convert better than generic content — because they connect directly to the core value the user has already experienced.

## What Good Looks Like
A product that has identified its "one thing" and built all re-engagement, notification, and onboarding flows around driving users toward that action. When a user becomes inactive, the re-engagement prompt is simple: "You haven't [done the one thing] in 2 weeks. Your [goal/streak/board] is waiting." Not "Explore 12 new features."

## Red Flags
- [ ] Retention strategy is "add features" rather than "deepen the core loop"
- [ ] No clear definition of the single action that predicts long-term retention
- [ ] Re-engagement emails showcase new features rather than returning to the core value
- [ ] Onboarding complexity has increased quarter-over-quarter as features were added
- [ ] Churned user analysis has not been conducted to identify the behavioural difference from retained users
