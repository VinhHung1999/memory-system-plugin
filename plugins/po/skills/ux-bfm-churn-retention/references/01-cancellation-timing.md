# Cancellation Timing

## Core Principle
By the time a user opens the cancellation screen, the decision to leave is already made. The "save the cancel" screen is not where retention happens — it is the symptom of a failure that occurred weeks or months earlier. The only effective retention intervention is upstream: before the user forms the intent to cancel.

## Diagnostic Questions
- At what point in the user journey does your retention content first appear? Is it during active use (upstream) or only on the cancel screen (downstream)?
- Does your cancel screen show personalised user data ("you completed 12 workouts this month") or generic feature lists ("access to 500+ workouts")?
- Do you offer a pause or downgrade option before the hard cancel? (Reduces irreversible loss)

## Design Checklist
- Surface goal-related data during active use — not just at cancellation
- Show the user's personal progress metrics throughout the app lifecycle, not only when they're leaving
- The cancel screen is a last resort — treat it as such. If it's doing heavy lifting, the problem is upstream
- Offer pause/downgrade before outright cancel to capture users who churn for price, not value reasons
- Never celebrate a "successful" cancel — make it feel like a loss without being manipulative

## Anti-Pattern
**What goes wrong:** Product teams design an elaborate "save the cancel" screen with testimonials, feature bullets, and a "Keep Subscription" button — and measure its conversion as a retention win.
**Why it fails:** The 5% of users who reverse on the cancel screen are not churning for value reasons. The 95% who proceed have already disengaged weeks earlier. Optimising the cancel screen is optimising the wrong metric.
**BFM Example (Strava):** The cancel screen shows impressive stats ("Subscribers stay twice as active, reach 54% more personal records") and goal progress. But this fires too late — the user already decided to cancel before opening Settings.

## BFM Evidence

### Strava — the decision is made before the cancel screen
> "Strava Subscribers stay twice as active and reach 54% more personal records."
> "Don't leave your goals behind!"

These are shown only on the cancel screen — but:

> "Partly because by this point the user has already made a decision to cancel."

### Strava — upstream goal-setting is the real retention mechanism
> "This is likely to make their retention hooks more effective. i.e., it's something you can commit to, and that they can motivate you with."

The insight: setting a goal during onboarding (e.g., "run 50 km this month") gives Strava a concrete hook to use throughout the lifecycle — not only at cancellation. The goal creates a reason to open the app tomorrow. The cancel screen benefits only if the goal was already active.

### Strava — transparent trial timeline reduces post-trial churn
> "Today: Unlock subscription features. 2 Days before: Get a reminder about when your trial will end. In 30 days: You'll be charged."

Pre-announcing the charge removes the "surprise billing" cancellation reason entirely. Users who proceed through a transparent trial are self-selected for value alignment.

## What Good Looks Like
A user who is considering cancellation has already received personalised progress updates weekly ("you hit a new personal record"), seen their streak, received a goal-progress notification, and been invited to a community challenge. By the time they open the cancel screen, they have 6 reasons to stay in their notification history — not just the cancel screen copy.

## Red Flags
- [ ] Cancel screen is the first time user sees their personal usage data
- [ ] No pause or downgrade option (forces binary stay/leave decision)
- [ ] "Save the cancel" copy lists features, not the user's personal history
- [ ] No upstream lifecycle moments that remind users of value before intent to cancel forms
- [ ] Trial end is not pre-announced — billing surprise is a top-3 cancellation reason
