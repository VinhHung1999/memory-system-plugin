# Pricing Psychology

## Core Principle
People don't evaluate prices in isolation — they evaluate them relative to other prices on the same screen. Adding, removing, or repositioning a tier can shift which option feels "sensible" without changing any price. The Decoy Effect and price anchoring are the two most reliable levers.

## Diagnostic Questions
- Does the range of prices on this screen anchor users toward the option you want them to choose?
- Is the "default" or highlighted option the one that is actually best for the user, or the one that is best for the business?
- Would removing one tier from this pricing table cause users to perceive remaining options differently?

## Design Checklist
- Anchor the comparison range so that your target tier sits in the "sensible middle"
- When introducing a new lower-cost tier, verify it does not cannibalise higher-margin plans
- Repeat technical specs in plain language so that different audience segments can make comparisons (e.g., both "1080p" and "HD quality")
- If a plan has a meaningful limitation, disclose it before the user pays — not after
- Avoid a confirmation screen only when you are deliberately preventing plan reconsideration

## Anti-Pattern
**What goes wrong:** A cheap "entry" tier is introduced to attract price-sensitive users but immediately cannibalises the mid-tier, reducing overall revenue.
**Why it happens:** Product teams model new tiers in isolation without accounting for how the full pricing range shifts anchor perception for all users.
**BFM Example:** Netflix — when Basic with Ads launched at £6.99, sign-ups for the cheapest tier jumped to 41% of new subscribers, pulling revenue away from Standard and Premium. Netflix resolved this by repricing Basic (ad-free) to £6.99 so that it appeared as a small upgrade over the ad tier, making Standard look more accessible by comparison.

## BFM Evidence
### Netflix — Decoy Effect: Basic tier cannibalises premium
> "And something super interesting happened. It cannibalised the more expensive tiers. i.e., whilst the basic plan grew in popularity... something about the value proposition had made the premium plans less popular, which was not what Netflix wanted to happen."

### Netflix — price range anchors the "sensible budget"
> "When there's only three options, most of the prices are above £10 per month. And so it doesn't feel unreasonable to spend above £10 on Netflix. 'That's just what it costs'. (Unless you're happy with the worst option)."

### Netflix — adding a fourth tier shifts the anchor
> "But having that fourth tier shifts the dynamic. Now, a parent buying Netflix for their family TV, has more optionality below £10. It feels like £10 could be the higher end of a sensible budget."

### Netflix — repeating specs serves different demographics
> "Would the average 70 year old be able to assign a suitable value (i.e., utility) on the upgrade from 1080p to 4K+HDR? (Unlikely). Netflix are repeating themselves here. And it's intentional, to add a non-technical way of comparing these options."

### Netflix — ruling out the ad tier drives Basic upgrade
> "Ah, see this is really clever. Now, the user has essentially ruled out this option being viable. It's way cheaper than everything else, which means it 'must be bad'."

### Flights — scarcity framing accelerates price decisions
> "Scarcity + urgency — inventory pressure combined with time limits forces quick decisions."

## What Good Looks Like
A pricing table where the range is deliberately calibrated: the lowest option has a visible drawback (ads, limited library, rock-hard mattress), the highest is clearly premium, and the target tier sits naturally in the "that seems reasonable" zone. Each tier's difference is explained in at least two ways — one technical, one human.

## Red Flags
- [ ] Adding a new lower-cost tier without checking whether it cannibalises the tier above it
- [ ] Pricing table anchors users toward the cheapest option rather than the target tier
- [ ] Plan limitations are disclosed only after the user has paid
- [ ] Technical specs appear in only one format (e.g., "4K" with no plain-language equivalent)
- [ ] A confirmation screen is removed to prevent users from reconsidering an upgrade
