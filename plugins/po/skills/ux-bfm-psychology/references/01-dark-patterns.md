# Dark Patterns

## Core Principle
Dark patterns are design choices that exploit cognitive biases to get users to do things against their own interests. The line between persuasion and manipulation is crossed when information is deliberately hidden, costs are revealed only after commitment, or urgency is fabricated rather than real.

## Diagnostic Questions
- Is every cost (subscription price, future charges, premium tier requirements) visible before the user commits?
- Is urgency or scarcity on this screen real — or is it a design choice to accelerate a decision?
- Does the user understand exactly what they are agreeing to when they tap the primary CTA?

## Design Checklist
- Show the real ongoing subscription price before payment, not just the discounted first-box price
- If an offer exists, keep it visible throughout the entire checkout — not just the first screen
- Use urgency only when the deadline is genuine (limited inventory, expiring sale)
- Label CTAs with the actual commitment ("I will deliver this" vs "Confirm")
- Provide a back button on every onboarding step so users can correct mistakes

## Anti-Pattern
**What goes wrong:** An offer is teased up front, then quietly reframed or hidden by the time the user reaches checkout, so they pay full price without realising it.
**Why it happens:** Teams optimise for conversion rate at each individual step rather than for long-term trust. Hiding the deal reduces cognitive load — but also informed consent.
**BFM Example:** HelloFresh — the "up to 60% off" offer shown on slide 1 was immediately reframed as "£80 in total," then vanished entirely from the registration and address pages. If the user clicked "Maybe Later," the offer was never shown again. They were charged full price at checkout.

## BFM Evidence
### HelloFresh — vague offer, no escape
> "Remember: the user hasn't seen the menu or prices. It's very early to assume that they're ready to commit to multiple orders."

### HelloFresh — offer disappears at the moment of rational decision-making
> "And yet, on these pages, they've hidden the deal. Which is a mistake, because this is their hook."

### HelloFresh — commitment obscured inside the flow
> "At a glance, you'd be very likely to make a subconscious assumption that all of these steps were encompassed within the flow. And that the commitment happens here. i.e., this is the point of no return. But it doesn't, it happens before that."

### HelloFresh — subscription cost never disclosed
> "To reiterate; this is a subscription, and at no point have you been told what your regular price will be."

### HelloFresh — two most common cancellation reasons
> "Is it a surprise then, that the two most common reasons for cancelling are that the prices are too high, and that people don't like the recipes? Both of those things are intentionally obscured prior to signing up."

### Flights — hidden costs revealed late
> "Hidden costs — ancillary fees only revealed late in funnel maximize conversion before abandonment."

## What Good Looks Like
Starling Bank's customer support: clear live-chat button, explicit wait time, human name on screen within one minute. Every commitment is labeled accurately. No bait-and-switch. The "good old days" of honest interaction — where what you see is what you get.

## Red Flags
- [ ] The ongoing subscription price is not shown before the payment screen
- [ ] An offer is mentioned on screen 1 but is absent from the checkout screen
- [ ] There is no back button during a multi-step onboarding with a payment at the end
- [ ] Urgency copy ("limited time", "only 2 seats left") has no verifiable basis
- [ ] The CTA label describes an action ("Confirm") rather than the commitment ("Subscribe for £28.99/month")
