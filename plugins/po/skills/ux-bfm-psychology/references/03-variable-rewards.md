# Variable Rewards

## Core Principle
Fixed rewards are predictable and quickly discounted; variable rewards generate anticipation because the outcome is unknown. A $7.08 bonus is more psychologically powerful than a $10 bonus because the non-round number signals genuine variability — and variability implies the next reward could be much larger. The act of choosing and claiming the reward, rather than receiving it automatically, amplifies the Endowment Effect.

## Diagnostic Questions
- Does this reward feel like it could be different each time, or does it feel like a flat discount?
- Does the user have to make a meaningful choice before claiming the reward, so they feel ownership over it?
- Is the feedback loop closed — can the user see their reward in context immediately after claiming it?

## Design Checklist
- Use a non-round reward amount to signal genuine variability (e.g., $7.08 rather than $10)
- Let the user choose which reward they receive rather than assigning it automatically
- Limit choice to a manageable set so users optimise rather than paralysing
- Close the feedback loop: show the claimed reward in the user's portfolio/account immediately
- Use animation or a physical ritual (scratch card, countdown) to build anticipation before reveal
- Prime for the next roll of the dice — establish the game mechanic before offering a referral bonus

## Anti-Pattern
**What goes wrong:** A welcome bonus is applied automatically as account credit, so it feels like a discount coupon rather than a reward. The user has no anticipation, no choice, no ownership — and no motivation to earn the next one.
**Why it happens:** The engineering path of least resistance is to credit the account directly. The psychology of the reward moment is treated as marketing, not product.
**BFM Example:** Robinhood (before regulatory action) — they understood the mechanic correctly but overdid it with confetti animations and digital scratch tickets, resulting in a $7.5M fine for gamifying investing. The lesson is not to remove the psychology, but to calibrate it.

## BFM Evidence
### Robinhood — the non-round number signals variability
> "This hook starts with an unusual reward of $7.08? A very random number, right? Well, this is a variable part of the reward. You might get $100, or you might get $1. Even if your chances of any meaningful bonus are very low... it adds a sense of anticipation and curiosity."

### Robinhood — $7.08 vs $10: the round number kills the magic
> "This 8 cents is a visual cue of that variability. If it were always $10, it might be less effective. Partly because the assumed value of the next box would likely also then be $10. As opposed to the irrationally higher expected value. (Fuelled on hopes and dreams)."

### Robinhood — forcing a choice creates the Endowment Effect
> "But instead, they force you to choose the stock that you want. It allows people to select the stock that they irrationally overvalue the most. At the end of this process, they want you to look at your portfolio and feel like it's personal to you. This is the Endowment Effect."

### Robinhood — choice without paralysis
> "But importantly there isn't too much choice. Too much choice would make this decision harder, and far more daunting. And lead to people trying to over-optimise their selection."

### Robinhood — the referral mechanic primes the next reward
> "Instead, they're priming you for the next step. They're establishing a game mechanic. And then offering you another 'roll of the dice'."

### Robinhood — the feedback loop must close
> "Usually in situations like this, you want to 'tie together' both parts of the experience. i.e., 'remember this free stock you received? And how rewarding it felt to become an investor? Well your stock is here now. Congratulations'."

## What Good Looks Like
A welcome bonus screen that shows a specific non-round dollar amount, offers a curated (not exhaustive) set of things to spend it on, requires one deliberate choice to claim, displays the claimed item prominently in the user's account immediately, and then — only then — surfaces the referral program as "another roll of the dice."

## Red Flags
- [ ] Welcome reward is a round number applied automatically as account credit
- [ ] The user has no choice in what they receive
- [ ] The claimed reward is buried in the account rather than surfaced immediately
- [ ] There is no animation, ritual, or build-up before the reward is revealed
- [ ] The referral program is presented before the user has experienced the first reward
