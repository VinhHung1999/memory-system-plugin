# Referral & Viral Mechanics

## Core Principle
Referral programmes fail when they treat sharing as a transaction ("give $10, get $10"). Sharing is a social act — people share what makes them look good, what surprises them, or what they genuinely believe will help someone they care about. A fixed, predictable reward removes all of these motivations. Variable rewards, genuine surprise, and social proof of completion are what make referral mechanics spread. The goal is not to incentivise sharing — it is to make sharing feel like the natural expression of having had a great experience.

## Diagnostic Questions
- Is your referral reward fixed and predictable (always the same amount, automatically applied)?
- Is the referral prompt surfaced at the moment of peak positive emotion — or in an account settings menu?
- Does the sharer receive a closed-loop notification when their referral completes ("your friend signed up — you earned £8.73")?
- Is the referral act positioned as a gift to the friend, or a transaction for the sharer?

## Design Checklist
- Use a non-round, variable reward amount — the variability creates anticipation and perceived irrationally high expected value
- Surface the referral at the moment of peak positive emotion (post-delivery, post-completion, post-win) — not in a settings menu
- Close the reward loop immediately when the referral converts: named push notification with the specific amount earned
- Frame the referral as giving something to a friend — not earning something for yourself
- Let the sharer choose how to claim their reward (apply to next order, save for later) — the choice creates ownership

## Anti-Pattern
**What goes wrong:** "$10 for you, $10 for your friend" referral programme sits in account settings. Almost nobody shares. The programme is still "active" because nobody has the authority to shut it down. It converts when the friend receives the code (concrete discount on a real purchase) but barely converts on the sharer side (abstract credit, no ritual, no surprise).
**Why it happens:** The programme was designed by someone who modelled the economics (if X users share and Y% convert, we break even at £Z). The psychological experience of sharing was not designed — only the economics.
**BFM Example (Robinhood):** Robinhood's referral works because the reward is not what you expect. A non-round number ($7.08, not $10) signals variability — "you might get $100, or you might get $1." This irrationally inflates the perceived expected value. Robinhood forces the recipient to choose which stock they want, creating ownership before they've received anything.

## BFM Evidence

### Robinhood — non-round reward signals variability
> "This hook starts with an unusual reward of $7.08? A very random number, right? Well, this is a variable part of the reward. You might get $100, or you might get $1. Even if your chances of any meaningful bonus are very low... it adds a sense of anticipation and curiosity."

> "This 8 cents is a visual cue of that variability. If it were always $10, it might be less effective. Partly because the assumed value of the next box would likely also then be $10. As opposed to the irrationally higher expected value. (Fuelled on hopes and dreams)."

### Robinhood — choice creates the Endowment Effect before receipt
> "But instead, they force you to choose the stock that you want. It allows people to select the stock that they irrationally overvalue the most. At the end of this process, they want you to look at your portfolio and feel like it's personal to you. This is the Endowment Effect."

The mechanics: before the user has received anything, they have made a choice. The act of choosing makes the reward feel already owned — making the referral more likely to be completed and the reward more highly valued.

### Robinhood — sequencing: experience reward first, then ask for referral
> "Instead, they're priming you for the next step. They're establishing a game mechanic. And then offering you another 'roll of the dice'."

> "Usually in situations like this, you want to 'tie together' both parts of the experience. i.e., 'remember this free stock you received? And how rewarding it felt to become an investor? Well your stock is here now. Congratulations'."

The sequence: user receives reward (variable stock) → user experiences the excitement → referral offer arrives, framed as "another roll of the dice." The referral is positioned as an extension of an already-positive experience, not a cold marketing ask.

### Monzo Perks — surfacing at the right emotional moment
The Monzo Perks case study reinforces timing: the moment a user discovers a perk that saves them money on something they already do is the peak motivation to share. "Look what I found" is a natural sharing impulse. A referral prompt surfaced at this moment converts better than the same prompt in an account settings menu.

## What Good Looks Like
A referral mechanic where: the sharer is prompted immediately after a high-emotion positive moment (delivery arrived, challenge completed, unexpected saving), the reward is variable and non-round, the sharer must make one active choice to claim it, and a closed-loop notification arrives the moment the friend completes their first action. The sharer remembers the moment — because it was memorable — and is primed to refer again.

## Red Flags
- [ ] Referral reward is a fixed round number applied automatically (no choice, no variability)
- [ ] Referral prompt lives in account settings — surfaced proactively never or only once in onboarding
- [ ] No closed-loop notification when the referred friend converts
- [ ] The referred friend receives more value from the referral than the sharer (broken motivation structure)
- [ ] Referral mechanics introduced as a campaign, not as a persistent product feature
