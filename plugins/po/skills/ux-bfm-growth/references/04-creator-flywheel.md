# Creator & Community Flywheel

## Core Principle
The most efficient growth engine is one where your best users grow the product for you. Creators who build an audience on your platform bring new users from outside your existing user base — users you could not have acquired through any paid channel. The flywheel works when: (1) creators get more value from being on your platform than anywhere else, (2) the platform makes it easy for creators to bring their audience in, and (3) new users who arrive through creators have a better experience than they would have had arriving cold. When the flywheel breaks, it's usually because the platform extracted more value than it gave to creators.

## Diagnostic Questions
- Do your most successful users publicly attribute their success to your platform?
- Is it easy for a creator to bring their existing audience to your platform? (Import subscribers, share link, one-click invite)
- Do new users who arrive through a creator recommendation have a better first experience than cold sign-ups?
- What does your platform give creators that they can't get elsewhere?

## Design Checklist
- Measure creator NPS separately from general user NPS — creator dissatisfaction is a leading indicator of flywheel breakdown
- Make it trivially easy for a creator to share a single-click subscription link to their audience
- New user signup that comes through a creator recommendation should be pre-personalised: show the creator's content first, not a generic feed
- Give creators data about their audience growth, engagement, and earnings — visibility into their success makes them advocates
- Don't unilaterally change monetisation rules for creators without warning — creator trust is the platform's most fragile asset

## Anti-Pattern
**What goes wrong:** Platform grows through creator content. Platform changes monetisation rules that reduce creator earnings (e.g., API changes that kill creator tools, revenue share changes). Top creators leave or reduce output. New user acquisition drops because the flywheel source has dried up. Platform increases paid acquisition to compensate, which is less efficient and more expensive.
**Why it happens:** Creator monetisation is owned by a revenue team. Creator satisfaction is nobody's KPI. The connection between creator health and new user acquisition is not visible in any single dashboard.
**BFM Example (Substack):** Substack's creator flywheel works because writers bring their email lists, their existing audiences, and their credibility. Each new writer is a distribution event — their announcement ("I'm moving to Substack") is Substack's most effective marketing.

## BFM Evidence

### Substack — creators as the acquisition channel
> "Imagine that someone has recommended a newsletter to you."

The scenario Substack is designed around: a subscriber arrives because a writer they already trust recommended them. The platform's job at this moment is to not break the trust transfer — get out of the way and let the creator's credibility convert the subscriber.

### Substack — single-click subscription to recommendations as flywheel
> "After this, you'll casually be given a single-click to subscribe to all of Lenny's recommendations."

> "Substack does know how to use dynamic CTAs. (Which makes a specific action like this clearer)."

The mechanic: when a user subscribes to one creator, they are offered single-click subscriptions to other creators that creator recommends. This is the flywheel in action: each creator brings new users who are then distributed to other creators on the platform, expanding everyone's audience simultaneously.

### Substack — obscuring the subscription path is the dark pattern version
> "Take a moment to think about their conundrum. If they were to even mention prices here, some people wouldn't bother subscribing to the free version."

Substack's tension: the creator flywheel works best when new subscribers have a frictionless entry. But Substack also wants paid conversions. The solution — vague language, removed navigation, default to paid plan — is effective at conversion but creates a trust problem with users who feel they were misled.

### Lovable — product-led growth through creator sharing
Lovable's growth case study: users who build and ship products with Lovable share what they built. Each shared product is a Lovable endorsement — "built with Lovable" attribution visible on the output. Users arrive not through ads but through seeing a product they liked, wanting to build something similar, and tracing it back to the tool.

This is the creator flywheel in a product-building context: the output of using the tool is also the advertisement for the tool.

## What Good Looks Like
A platform where the top 100 creators are actively and publicly growing their audiences, regularly tell their audiences they are on your platform, and recommend other creators on your platform. New users arrive pre-warm — they know the creator, trust their recommendation, and have a reason to stay beyond the platform's own value proposition. Each new creator brings a distribution event that no paid ad can replicate.

## Red Flags
- [ ] Creator NPS not measured separately from general user NPS
- [ ] Creators must leave the platform to tell their audience they've joined
- [ ] Platform changes monetisation rules without creator consultation
- [ ] New users who arrive through creator referral see the same generic cold-start experience as any other new user
- [ ] "Built with [platform]" attribution is opt-in, hard to find, or absent from outputs
