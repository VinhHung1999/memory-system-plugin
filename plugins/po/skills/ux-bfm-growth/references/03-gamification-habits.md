# Gamification & Habit Loops

## Core Principle
Gamification fails when it is designed as a veneer — points and badges on top of a product that doesn't need them. It works when it solves a real motivational problem: the gap between starting something and seeing meaningful results. The best gamification doesn't feel like a game — it feels like progress. It structures a long-term behaviour into stages, solves the "middle slump" (where novelty has worn off but the goal feels distant), and delivers a meaningful reward at the end that makes the user want to talk about it.

## Diagnostic Questions
- What is the motivational problem your gamification is solving? (Starting? Middle slump? Finishing?)
- Is there a "nearly there" moment — where proximity to the goal creates its own urgency?
- Does completing the challenge produce a social moment (something the user wants to share)?
- What happens the day after the challenge ends? Is there a natural next loop?

## Design Checklist
- Structure long challenges into clearly labelled stages (The Easy Bit / The Hard Bit / Nearly There)
- Solve the middle slump with intermediate milestones and proximity goals, not just daily reminders
- End the challenge with a completion moment that is shareable and emotionally resonant — not just a notification
- Design the "day after" — what does the user do once the challenge is complete?
- Don't rely on novelty alone; novelty lasts 1-2 weeks. Sustainable motivation requires structural design

## Anti-Pattern
**What goes wrong:** A year-long savings challenge is launched with a great onboarding experience. Engagement is high for weeks 1-4 (novelty). It drops precipitously in weeks 8-20 (middle slump — novelty gone, goal feels distant). A burst of re-engagement happens in the final weeks (proximity effect). The team attributes the re-engagement to their notification campaign. They copy it for next year's challenge. Middle slump returns.
**Why it happens:** The team optimised for launch excitement and end-point conversion. The middle — which is where most of the product lifetime occurs — was not designed.
**BFM Example (Monzo 1p Savings Challenge):** Monzo identified the three motivational stages explicitly and designed different mechanics for each. The "Easy Bit" (early days) runs on novelty. "The Hard Bit" (middle, weeks 8-20) is kept alive with intermediate milestones and proximity goals that make the daily save feel meaningful. "Nearly There" activates proximity urgency in the final stretch.

## BFM Evidence

### Monzo — three-stage motivation framework
> "And it turns out that we can break the year down into 3 stages: THE EASY BIT / THE HARD BIT THAT PEOPLE IGNORE / NEARLY THERE."

> "(Most people ignore this one)." [referring to the middle stage]

The key insight: most gamification design invests in stage 1 (exciting launch mechanics) and stage 3 (completion celebration). Stage 2 — the long, boring middle — is where users are lost and where deliberate design has the most impact.

### Monzo — proximity goals solve the middle slump
> "This lasts an entire year, so it can't be powered by novelty alone. I was interested to see how Monzo would keep people motivated for that long."

The mechanism: rather than showing "£675 to go" (a discouraging distance), the interface shows proximity to the next intermediate milestone. Users are always "nearly there" for something, even if the end goal is months away. This activates the goal gradient effect: effort increases as the goal approaches.

### Monzo — completion creates word-of-mouth
The challenge completion screen is designed to be shareable: the user has a concrete achievement (saved £675.45 in a year) with a specific, remarkable number. "I saved £675.45 doing the 1p challenge" is a conversation-starter. This is the growth flywheel: gamification creates both the behaviour AND the word-of-mouth event.

> "You did it, Peter! You started with 1p and saved £675.45. Enjoy your savings."

The specific amount (not "you saved £600+") is important — it's personal, exact, and surprising. It's the variable reward equivalent in a savings context.

### Duolingo comparison — streak as the core retention mechanic
The Monzo case study references Duolingo's streak as a benchmark: the streak creates daily urgency through loss aversion (breaking a 47-day streak feels like losing something real). Monzo applies the same principle to savings: each daily save maintains a sense of momentum that makes skipping feel like a loss.

## What Good Looks Like
A year-long challenge where: the first week runs on excitement and simplicity ("save 1p today"), weeks 2-10 are structured around intermediate milestones that always keep the user "nearly there" for something, the final month activates proximity urgency, and the completion produces a shareable, specific, personal achievement that users bring up unprompted in conversation.

## Red Flags
- [ ] Gamification design ends at "points and badges" — no stage-appropriate mechanics
- [ ] No intermediate milestones between launch and end goal
- [ ] Middle engagement drop interpreted as a notification problem, not a design problem
- [ ] Completion moment is a generic notification ("Challenge complete!") with no social sharing element
- [ ] No "day after" design — what does the user do when the challenge ends?
