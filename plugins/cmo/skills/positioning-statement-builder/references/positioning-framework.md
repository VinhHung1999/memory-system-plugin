# Positioning Framework — IE Business School Methodology

This reference is the load-on-demand backbone for the `positioning-statement-builder` skill. It captures the IE methodology end-to-end: how positioning fits the broader marketing process, the 3-element positioning statement, frame-of-reference discipline, the 3-filter Point of Difference validator, the Urbany-Davis 3-circles diagnostic, the 5 differentiation levers, and the alternative positioning playbook (reverse / breakaway / stealth).

---

## Section 1 — The IE Marketing Strategy Framework

Marketing strategy at IE is taught as a strict three-step sequence. Skipping or reordering steps produces positioning statements that look complete but cannot guide real decisions.

1. **5Cs — Value ASSESSMENT.** Customers, Company, Competitors, Collaborators, Context. Diagnoses the playing field. Outputs: segment shortlist, competitor map, contextual constraints (regulation, technology shifts, cultural moment).
2. **Positioning — Value CREATION.** Picks the segment, defines the value proposition, fixes the frame of reference. Outputs: a 3-element positioning statement with at least one validated Point of Difference (PoD) and the Reasons to Believe (RTBs) that support it.
3. **4Ps — Value DELIVERY & CAPTURE.** Product, Price, Place, Promotion. Translates the positioning into concrete commercial mechanics. Outputs: launch plan, pricing band, channel mix, campaign brief, KPIs.

### Why each step must precede the next

- Doing 4Ps without positioning produces incoherent campaigns — pricing fights promotion, channel fights product.
- Doing positioning without 5Cs produces fictional segments — you commit to a target you've never measured.
- Doing 5Cs without positioning produces decks — analysis with no commitment.

**Positioning is the hinge.** It is where assessment becomes commitment. A correct positioning statement can survive multiple 4Ps revisions; a wrong one corrupts every downstream tactical decision.

## Section 2 — The 3-Element Positioning Statement

Every positioning statement at IE must contain exactly these three elements. Templates that omit one are incomplete. Templates that add more dilute focus.

### Element 1 — Target Segment

Specific. Named. Evidenced.

- **Segment description:** Who, in how many words it takes to be unambiguous. "Mid-market finance teams ($50M-$500M ARR) running Sage, NetSuite, or QuickBooks" beats "growth-stage finance teams."
- **Segment characteristics (MAPDA test):** Measurable, Accessible, Profitable, Differentiable, Actionable. If a segment fails any of these, the positioning will misfire.
- **Segment evidence:** What data or customer voice justified the choice? Survey result, win-loss record, qualitative interview themes. "Internal hunch" is not evidence.

### Element 2 — Value Proposition

Two parts: Points of Parity (PoPs) — the must-haves to be considered — and Points of Difference (PoDs) — the distinctive elements that make customers choose.

- **PoPs (table stakes):** Per Kevin Lane Keller, two flavors: (a) **category PoPs** — generic must-haves (a car must have ABS, AC, central locking); (b) **competitive PoPs** — chosen specifically to neutralize a rival's claim (McDonald's salads aren't about entering healthy food; they're about reducing the rejecter pool).
- **PoDs (chosen battlefields):** 1-3 distinctive claims, each passing the 3-filter check (see Section 5). PoDs can sit at three levels: **attribute** (ingredient, ABS, retina display), **benefit** (saves time cooking, faster recovery), **value** (cares for your family, makes you brave). Developed-market positioning leans toward benefits and values.

### Element 3 — Frame of Reference

This is the single most-failed element. Frame of reference is **the customer NEED COVERED, not the product category.**

- **Wrong:** "the leading CRM software for sales teams" (category-anchored — locks you into competing with other CRMs only).
- **Right:** "the unified pipeline visibility for revenue leaders" (need-anchored — opens competition vs spreadsheets, BI tools, ops consultants).

### Worked Example — Citroën

When Citroën launched the C4 Cactus, the team explicitly framed against the urban-mobility need (frequent low-speed bumps, parking damage), not the SUV category. The positioning statement combined:
- **Target:** urban families who cared more about practicality than status.
- **Value prop:** Airbump panels (PoD — protected against parking dings) + spacious cabin (PoP).
- **Frame of reference:** "stress-free urban driving," not "C-segment crossover."

The Airbump PoD was distinctive (no competitor had it), relevant (the segment consistently named parking damage as a top frustration), and trustworthy (the panels were physically demonstrable).

## Section 3 — Frame of Reference: Need vs Category

The "frame by need" rule comes from observing the difference between brands that grew the category and brands that stayed locked inside one.

### The Starbucks Pattern

Howard Schultz called Starbucks "the third place" — between home and work. Starbucks does not compete with cafes; it competes with **bars, cinemas, restaurants, hotel lobbies** — anywhere that satisfies the "third place" need. This frame:
- Justifies the larger square footage and seating density (cafes don't do this).
- Justifies music programs, free Wi-Fi, longer dwell time (cafes don't).
- Justifies premium prices (cafes can't).

Reframing from "premium coffee" to "third place need" was what unlocked the strategic playbook.

### The Tesco Pattern

Tesco frames against "one-stop family shopping," which puts it in competition with weekend markets, online grocers, and even meal-kit subscriptions — not just supermarkets. That frame justified clothing aisles, in-store banks, in-store opticians, mobile-phone aisles. None of those make sense if the frame is "supermarket."

### Why Category-Framing Limits Strategy

A category frame inherits the category's PoPs, the category's pricing band, and the category's customer expectations. It also inherits the category's competitor set, which means every PoD must out-do an established player. Switching to a need frame:
- Unlocks adjacencies (Starbucks merch, Starbucks reserve roasteries).
- Allows higher pricing (because the comparison set shifts).
- Repels weak competitors (they're not built for the need, only the category).

### Decision Tree

- If the category is mature, crowded, and price-pressured → widen frame to need.
- If the brand is unknown and needs orientation → narrow frame to a familiar category, then widen later.
- If a regulator or buyer-RFP locks the category vocabulary → keep the category frame externally but plan strategy around the need frame internally.

## Section 4 — Points of Parity vs Points of Difference

PoPs and PoDs are not interchangeable. Order matters.

### PoPs

The minimum bundle a customer expects before considering you. If you fail a PoP, you're disqualified before the PoD ever gets considered. PoPs are usually invisible when present and fatal when missing.

Two failure modes:
- **Aspirational PoPs:** treating an industry-wide weakness as table stakes ("everyone offers SOC 2") — fine, but verify it's actually expected by THIS segment.
- **Trapped-by-category PoPs:** carrying PoPs from a category you're trying to escape. If you reframe to a new need, audit and prune PoPs that don't apply.

### PoDs

The distinctive elements that make customers actively choose you. PoDs survive two questions:
1. Would the customer notice if it disappeared? (relevance test)
2. Would the customer struggle to get this elsewhere? (exclusivity test)

If you can't say yes to both, it's not a PoD; it's a feature on a roadmap.

### Order Matters

Customers compare on PoDs only after PoPs are satisfied. A killer PoD on a product missing a PoP loses every time. This is why "differentiation" without parity is a common failure: you build the cool feature but lose the deal because integrations don't work.

### PoPs vs PoDs Drift

Markets shift. Yesterday's PoD becomes today's PoP. When Netflix launched, "stream on any device" was a PoD; today it's a PoP. Re-audit the PoP/PoD list every 12-18 months.

## Section 5 — The 3-Filter PoD Validator

Every claimed PoD must pass three filters. Fail any one → revise or replace.

### Filter 1 — RELEVANT

**Definition:** the PoD addresses a need that the chosen segment actually has and ranks high.

**How to test:**
- Cross-reference the PoD against the segment's pain inventory from 5Cs research.
- Ask: would a customer in this segment volunteer this benefit when describing what they want?
- Run the inverse test: would removing this attribute cause a measurable drop in interest from the segment?

**Common failure modes:**
- PoD addresses a real pain, but for a different segment.
- PoD addresses a pain the segment has but doesn't prioritize (it's a "nice").
- PoD is internally interesting (engineering achievement) but invisible to customers.

**Cheapest verification:** show 5 customers two product descriptions (with PoD vs without) and ask which they'd buy. If they don't notice or don't care, fail.

### Filter 2 — EXCLUSIVE

**Definition:** the PoD is distinctive vs current alternatives within the chosen frame of reference.

**How to test:**
- Map the same claim across competitor websites. If anyone else makes the same claim with similar specificity, exclusivity is gone.
- Apply the "leading / best / fastest" test: a generic superlative without a benchmark is automatically not exclusive.
- Test against analogous categories: would a substitute-category competitor (in the need-frame) also be able to claim this?

**Common failure modes:**
- Generic superlatives ("the leading X for Y").
- Claims of personality traits ("we care more").
- Claims that are technically true but not perceptually distinctive.

**Cheapest verification:** read your top 3 competitors' homepage taglines. If yours could swap with theirs without anyone noticing, fail.

### Filter 3 — TRUSTWORTHY

**Definition:** there is at least one Reason to Believe (RTB) that supports the claim — third-party validation, measurable benchmark, demonstrable mechanism.

**How to test:**
- Force the question: "what specifically do we point to when a skeptical customer asks for proof?"
- Reject "trust me" answers. Reject internal NPS as RTB (unless audited externally).
- Numbers + comparison are the gold standard ("12s avg vs 45s industry").

**Common failure modes:**
- RTB is itself a claim ("we're known for quality").
- RTB is an unaudited customer survey.
- RTB is a single anecdote.

**Cheapest verification:** if you can't write the RTB into a footnote with a citation, it's not an RTB.

## Section 6 — The Three Circles Model (Urbany & Davis, HBR 2007)

A visual diagnostic for where you stand vs customer needs and competitor offerings — all from the **customer's perceptual lens**, not internal description.

### The Three Circles

- **Circle A — Customer needs and wishes (within segment).** Both explicit (what customers say) and latent (what they don't articulate but feel). Latent needs are where breakthrough PoDs live.
- **Circle B — Your offering as customers PERCEIVE it.** Critical word: perceive. What you intend doesn't matter; what they encode matters.
- **Circle C — Competitor offering as customers PERCEIVE it.** Same lens.

### The 6 Zones

Drawing the circles produces six zones. Each implies a strategic action.

1. **Sweet spot — A ∩ B - C (you uniquely cover a real need).** Lead the positioning statement here. Amplify in messaging. Prioritize on the roadmap.
2. **Surrender zone — A ∩ B ∩ C (everyone covers it).** Commodity. Accept as PoP or push past it.
3. **Equity at risk — B - A - C (you cover something nobody needs).** Wasted spend. Kill it or reposition.
4. **Opportunity — A - B - C (unmet need nobody covers).** Highest-leverage PoD candidate. Validate demand, then build.
5. **Competitor strength — A ∩ C - B (they cover a need; you don't).** Decide: catch up, neutralize via PoP, or concede the segment slice.
6. **Competitor waste — C - A - B (they cover something nobody needs).** Note for intel; don't repeat their mistake.

### How to Source the Data

| Circle | Best methods |
|---|---|
| A — needs | Customer interviews (latent needs), JTBD studies, rejection/win-loss interviews, social listening |
| B — your perception | Customer perception studies, NPS verbatims, sales-call recordings, review-mining |
| C — competitor perception | Competitor review-mining (G2, Capterra, App Store), win-loss vs them, switcher interviews |

Rule: never source any circle from internal opinion. The whole model breaks if Circle B reflects how you describe yourself.

## Section 7 — 5 Differentiation Strategies

Once a PoD is chosen, decide WHICH lever produces the differentiation. IE teaches five.

### Lever 1 — Product Attributes

Tangible features: form, components, design, packaging, ingredients. Includes differentiation by SUBTRACTION (no additives, simplified phone for elderly users, KIA's 7-year warranty).

**Examples:** Apple (industrial design + integrated hardware/software), Dyson (motor technology), Nespresso (capsule format).

**Pick when:** you have a defensible product or design edge that translates into customer-perceptible differences.

### Lever 2 — Services

Ease of purchase, delivery, installation, customer support, maintenance.

**Examples:** Tesco home delivery, Amazon Prime same-day, Zappos returns.

**Pick when:** the underlying product is parity but the experience around it isn't.

### Lever 3 — Staff / People

Competence, courtesy, credibility, problem-solving. Universal applicability — even identical products at identical prices become differentiated by call-center quality.

**Examples:** Vodafone Spain customer service, Ritz-Carlton staff empowerment, Singapore Airlines cabin crew.

**Pick when:** human touch is part of the product experience and your operation can sustain hiring + training.

### Lever 4 — R&D / Technology

Patents, proprietary technology, computational edge.

**Examples:** Tesla (battery + autopilot), pharma drug pipelines, Google search ranking.

**Pick when:** you have or can build durable IP and the market values cutting-edge.

### Lever 5 — Image / Brand

Symbolism, emotional connection, brand values.

**Examples:** Coca-Cola (happiness), Apple (creativity + status), Nike (achievement).

**Pick when:** functional parity dominates and emotional differentiation is the last open lever — usually mature categories.

### When to Pick Which

- Early-stage / new category → Product or R&D.
- Mid-stage / growing → Services or Staff.
- Mature / commodified → Image / Brand.
- Defensive plays → Services + Staff (hardest to copy).

Price and Distribution can also act as differentiators but only as **second-phase** levers — set after the core value prop is locked.

## Section 8 — Alternative Positioning Strategies

When the standard playbook (more features, sharper PoD) is exhausted, three alternative strategies open new ground.

### Reverse Positioning — REMOVE taken-for-granted attributes

The category teaches customers to expect a bundle. You strip elements out and reframe what's left.

**Example: IKEA.** Removed personalized in-store support, removed home delivery (the customer takes flat-pack home), removed assembly (the customer assembles). Left a price advantage that's visually striking. The "missing" attributes look like a reason to lower the price — even though customers eventually re-buy delivery and assembly as add-ons.

**Example: JetBlue (early days).** Removed first class. Reinvested in TVs at every seat. Cheaper bundle, distinctive in-flight experience.

**When it works:** the category has bundled attributes that customers don't actually value as highly as the bundle price suggests. Strip out the lowest-value, highest-cost attributes.

**Risks:** if customers value the removed attribute more than research suggested, you lose the deal silently (they won't tell you why they didn't choose).

### Breakaway Positioning (Blue Ocean) — REDEFINE the category itself

You take elements from one category and combine them with elements from another, producing something the category labels can't describe. The competitor set becomes ambiguous, and price becomes elastic.

**Example: Cirque du Soleil.** Kept the tent, the clowns, the artistry from circus. Added the refinement and continuous narrative relaunch from theater. Dropped animals (high cost, ethical risk) and famous-name artists (high cost). Result: higher perceived value AND lower cost structure. Profits boosted from both directions.

**Example: Dollar Shave Club.** Combined razor product with subscription-commerce delivery. Frame shifted from "razor brand" to "household consumables on autopilot."

**When it works:** the existing category is locked in red-ocean trade-offs (higher value = higher cost). Breakaway escapes that line.

**Risks:** the new frame can confuse customers initially. Requires patient education or strong PR push.

### Stealth Positioning — HIDE negative associations

The product is good, but the obvious category association would trigger rejection. So you position into a different category.

**Example: Sony Aibo.** Positioned as an entertainment robot, NOT a toy (toys for adults trigger embarrassment), NOT a pet replacement (provokes rejection from pet owners). The framing kept buyers comfortable with the purchase.

**Example: hard seltzer.** Positioned as "lifestyle beverage" rather than "low-cal beer," dodging "diet beer" stigma.

**When it works:** the category is correct but stigmatized. A new frame removes the stigma without misleading customers.

**Risks:** ethical line — stealth positioning can shade into deception. Stay on the safe side: the new frame must be substantively true, not just disguising.

## Section 9 — Positioning Maps + Spider Web Perceptual Maps

Visual tools for diagnosing competitive position. Both must be sourced from CUSTOMER research, never internal opinion.

### 2D Positioning Maps

Plot brands on two attributes (price vs quality, complexity vs simplicity, mass vs premium). Useful when:
- Two attributes dominate purchase decision.
- The map reveals empty quadrants (potential blue oceans) or crowded quadrants (red oceans to escape).

**Caveat:** picking the two axes matters. Choose attributes the customer actually weighs. Internal teams default to attributes engineers care about, which produce useless maps.

### Spider Web Perceptual Maps

Plot brands on 3-8 attributes simultaneously, producing a polygon per brand. Useful when:
- Decision is multidimensional.
- You want to see attribute-by-attribute strength/weakness vs each competitor.

**How to read:** a brand whose polygon dominates yours on 4+ axes has a structural advantage. A brand whose polygon doesn't overlap yours at all may be in a different segment.

### Discipline

- Always ground in research (n ≥ 30 in each customer segment).
- Re-run every 12-18 months; perceptions drift.
- Pair with the 3-circles model: maps tell you where you stand, 3-circles tells you what to do about it.

## Section 10 — Common Positioning Failure Modes

Patterns that show up in audits. Each fails one or more of the IE checks.

### 1. Generic Positioning ("the leading X for Y")

Symptoms: superlatives without benchmarks, swap-test passes (your statement = competitor's statement with brand swapped).
Fix: replace generic terms with measurable, specific PoDs.

### 2. Internal-Feature-List Positioning

Symptoms: feature list, no benefit translation, no segment voice.
Fix: convert each feature into a customer-language outcome. Apply the 3-level PoD rule (attribute → benefit → value) and pick benefit or value.

### 3. Frame-by-Category Lock-in

Symptoms: competitor set defined by category vocabulary, growth strategy stuck inside the category, pricing fights are zero-sum.
Fix: re-frame against the underlying need. Test by asking, "what else would the customer hire if we didn't exist?"

### 4. Speculative PoDs Without RTB

Symptoms: PoDs exist but team can't answer "why should anyone believe?". Marketing asks engineering for "validation."
Fix: every PoD gets at least 1 RTB, and at least 1 RTB must be third-party or measurable.

### 5. Inconsistent 4Ps That Contradict Positioning

Symptoms: premium positioning + discount channel mix; budget positioning + boutique distribution; differentiated PoD + me-too messaging.
Fix: walk every P (Product, Price, Place, Promotion) against the positioning statement. Each must reinforce the PoD or, at minimum, not contradict it. Run the Samsung/Ryanair stress test from Section 1: would the chosen 4Ps still make sense under the opposite positioning?

### 6. Audience Drift

Symptoms: positioning written for "everyone interested in X." Sales finds the actual buyers don't fit, but product roadmap stays generic.
Fix: re-narrow the segment using MAPDA (Measurable / Accessible / Profitable / Differentiable / Actionable). Re-source segment evidence.

### 7. Confusing Positioning With Tagline

Symptoms: the positioning statement IS the tagline. No internal guidance document exists.
Fix: keep the positioning statement internal (3 elements + RTBs); pick a tagline angle (benefit, competition, company-context, customer, image) for external use, derived FROM the positioning, not equal to it.

## Quick Reference — Stress-Test Checklist

When auditing any positioning statement, run these tests:

- [ ] Target segment is specific and evidenced (not "everyone interested in X").
- [ ] Frame of reference is by NEED, not category.
- [ ] Each PoD passes Relevant + Exclusive + Trustworthy.
- [ ] Each PoD has at least 1 concrete RTB.
- [ ] PoP list is real (not aspirational) and current (not legacy).
- [ ] Statement reads in 60 seconds.
- [ ] Statement guides 4Ps decisions (kill / scale / scope).
- [ ] Statement passes the swap test (won't work if you swap your brand for a competitor's).
- [ ] Differentiation lever is named (product / services / staff / R&D / image).
- [ ] Alternative strategies considered (reverse / breakaway / stealth) and rejected with reasoning OR adopted.
