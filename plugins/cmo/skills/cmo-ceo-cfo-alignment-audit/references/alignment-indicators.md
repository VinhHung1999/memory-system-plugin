# CMO ⇄ CEO ⇄ CFO Alignment Indicators — Reference

Comprehensive indicator catalog for the trio alignment audit. Read this when interpreting an indicator's score, when designing a remediation, or when defending the audit's methodology to a skeptical executive.

---

## Section 1 — The McKinsey Trio Thesis

### Why CMO-CEO-CFO alignment matters

McKinsey research (cited across the CMO Excellence curriculum and reinforced in HBR) shows that companies where the CMO is formally aligned with both the CEO and CFO outperform peers on top-line growth — sometimes by 2x or more on sustained revenue gain. The mechanism is structural, not cultural: the trio creates a closed feedback loop between strategy (CEO), capital (CFO), and customer reality (CMO). When that loop is intact, growth bets are evaluated on customer evidence + capital efficiency simultaneously. When it is broken, growth bets get evaluated on whichever lens is loudest in the room.

### The "trio" pattern vs the "lone CMO" pattern

The **trio pattern** looks like:
- The CMO has a standing weekly 1:1 with the CEO.
- The CMO has co-built at least one model with a CFO finance analyst.
- All three can articulate the company's top 3 marketing bets in the same language.
- Budget conversations happen as investment-thesis reviews, not as cost negotiations.

The **lone CMO pattern** looks like:
- The CMO is invited to exec sync as a guest, not a peer.
- The CFO treats marketing attribution as advocacy, not analysis.
- The CEO can name marketing's slogan but not its top 3 metrics.
- Budget cuts arrive without consultation.

Most CMOs *think* they are in the trio pattern. The audit is designed to surface where they are actually in the lone-CMO pattern.

### What changes when alignment is weak

- **Budget volatility.** Marketing budget swings 15-30 percent year over year because there is no shared model for what marketing buys.
- **Strategic invisibility.** Pivots happen without marketing input, then marketing is asked to "amplify" a strategy it did not shape.
- **Attribution wars.** Every quarter ends with finance and marketing arguing about what drove the number.
- **CMO tenure compression.** The average CMO tenure (under 4 years) tracks closely with the absence of formal trio rituals.

The audit exists because most CMOs do not realize they are in this pattern until they receive the budget cut, the pivot announcement, or the board-feedback letter.

## Section 2 — CEO Alignment Indicators

### CEO-1: Cadence of CMO-CEO 1:1s in last 90 days

**Diagnostic question:** Cadence of CMO-CEO 1:1s in the last 90 days?

**Why this matters:** 1:1 cadence is the single most predictive structural variable. Weekly 1:1s create the surface area for marketing to shape strategy in small increments before big decisions are made. Monthly or sporadic cadence means marketing gets briefed on decisions, not consulted before them.

**What healthy / yellow / red look like:**
- 🟢 Weekly, never cancelled. CEO protects the slot.
- 🟡 Bi-weekly, mostly held. Drifts during quarter-end.
- 🔴 Monthly or ad-hoc. CMO is reactive, not co-shaping.

**Common rationalization:** "We talk all the time in other meetings — formal 1:1s are unnecessary." This conflates *exposure* with *deliberate strategic conversation*. Hallway chats do not surface the gnarly questions a structured 1:1 forces.

**Cheapest first-week move:** Send a calendar invite for a recurring 30-minute weekly slot with a 3-bullet agenda template. Do not ask permission — propose it as the new default. Most CEOs accept structured time more readily than unstructured time.

---

### CEO-2: Whether CEO can articulate marketing's top 3 metrics WITHOUT looking

**Diagnostic question:** Can the CEO articulate marketing's top 3 metrics without looking at notes?

**Why this matters:** This is a recall test for what the CEO has actually internalized. If the CEO cannot name your top 3 metrics, those metrics do not exist at the exec scoreboard level — which means marketing is being judged on whatever the CEO does remember (usually a brand-awareness anecdote or last quarter's miss).

**What healthy / yellow / red look like:**
- 🟢 Names all 3, links each to revenue.
- 🟡 Names 2 of 3, or names 3 but cannot link to revenue.
- 🔴 Names vanity metrics or none.

**Common rationalization:** "Our CEO is non-marketing-native, so I do not expect them to know the metrics." This is exactly the problem. A non-marketing-native CEO without the metrics will substitute intuition or politics when judging marketing.

**Cheapest first-week move:** Pick the 3 metrics, write them on a single page with current value + target + last-quarter trend, and walk the CEO through it in the next 1:1. Then re-test recall in 30 days.

---

### CEO-3: Whether marketing has a seat in weekly exec sync

**Diagnostic question:** Does marketing have a permanent seat in the weekly exec sync?

**Why this matters:** Standing presence in the exec sync is structural permission to participate in cross-functional decisions. A "guest seat" model means marketing is invited only when the topic is already framed as marketing-relevant — by which point the framing is locked.

**What healthy / yellow / red look like:**
- 🟢 Standing seat with a recurring speaking slot.
- 🟡 Seat without consistent agenda slot.
- 🔴 Invited only when topic is marketing-adjacent.

**Common rationalization:** "Our exec sync is operations-heavy and marketing topics rarely come up." This is the rationalization to walk into the trap. Operations-heavy syncs are *exactly* where customer signal is most absent — and most needed.

**Cheapest first-week move:** Bring one customer-backed insight to the next exec sync that reframes an operational decision (e.g., a churn signal that bears on a pricing question). Earn the seat by demonstrating the absence costs the team.

---

### CEO-4: Whether last strategic pivot consulted CMO BEFORE the decision

**Diagnostic question:** On the last strategic pivot, were you consulted before the decision?

**Why this matters:** Pivot consultation is a definitive test of whether the CMO is structurally inside the strategy loop or downstream of it. A CMO informed during the decision meeting is a stakeholder, not a co-architect.

**What healthy / yellow / red look like:**
- 🟢 Input shaped the pivot.
- 🟡 Consulted in parallel with other functions.
- 🔴 Found out after.

**Common rationalization:** "The pivot was a finance-driven decision so marketing input was not relevant." Every pivot has customer implications — pricing, positioning, segment, lifecycle. If marketing was not in the room, customer evidence was not in the room.

**Cheapest first-week move:** Schedule a 1:1 to debrief the pivot specifically — what evidence was used, what customer questions were raised, what role marketing should have played. Do this even if the pivot was 6 months ago. Establishes the precedent for the next one.

---

### CEO-5: Whether CMO has standing slot in board meeting agenda

**Diagnostic question:** Do you have a standing slot in the board meeting agenda?

**Why this matters:** Board exposure tests whether the CEO is *willing* to give marketing voice at the highest decision-making layer. A CMO who only presents when asked to is presenting a topic, not a function.

**What healthy / yellow / red look like:**
- 🟢 Standing slot every board meeting.
- 🟡 Quarterly or every other board meeting.
- 🔴 Only when asked, or never.

**Common rationalization:** "Our board is product- and finance-focused — there is no marketing slot to fill." Boards reflect what the CEO emphasizes. The absence of a marketing slot is a signal about how the CEO frames marketing.

**Cheapest first-week move:** Propose to the CEO a 5-minute "customer pulse" recurring slot — not a marketing presentation, but a customer evidence summary. Easier to land than a full slot.

---

### CEO-6: Whether CEO public-facing quotes about marketing reflect actual marketing strategy

**Diagnostic question:** Do the CEO's public-facing quotes about marketing reflect your actual strategy?

**Why this matters:** What the CEO says about marketing externally is the leakiest signal of what they actually believe internally. Generic platitudes signal the CMO has not equipped the CEO with the right language.

**What healthy / yellow / red look like:**
- 🟢 On-message, uses your framing.
- 🟡 Mostly aligned with occasional drift.
- 🔴 Generic or off-message.

**Common rationalization:** "The CEO's communications team handles their messaging." That team will default to generic marketing quotes unless you give them better ones.

**Cheapest first-week move:** Send the CEO's chief of staff a short doc with 3 on-message quote options about marketing's strategy. Make it trivially easy to use your language.

## Section 3 — CFO Alignment Indicators

### CFO-1: Whether CFO trusts marketing attribution model

**Diagnostic question:** Does the CFO trust your marketing attribution model?

**Why this matters:** Attribution trust is the gating variable for every budget conversation. Without it, the CFO defaults to "show me the spend" because they cannot evaluate "show me the return."

**What healthy / yellow / red look like:**
- 🟢 CFO defends the model in board meetings.
- 🟡 Trusts privately, will not vouch externally.
- 🔴 Openly distrusts.

**Common rationalization:** "Our attribution is industry-standard — the CFO just does not understand it." If the CFO does not understand it, it is not built right. The CFO should be able to walk a board through your attribution in 90 seconds.

**Cheapest first-week move:** Ask the CFO what would have to be true for them to defend the model publicly. Their answer is the spec for the next iteration.

---

### CFO-2: Whether budget allocation is opportunity-based vs "last year + X percent"

**Diagnostic question:** How is marketing budget allocated this year?

**Why this matters:** "Last year plus X" budgeting means marketing is being managed as a cost center. Opportunity-based budgeting (each bet sized by expected return + risk + payback) means marketing is being managed as capital allocation.

**What healthy / yellow / red look like:**
- 🟢 Opportunity-based with investment thesis per bet.
- 🟡 Mostly opportunity-based with legacy carry-over.
- 🔴 Last-year-plus or finance-imposed cuts.

**Common rationalization:** "Our company is too small for opportunity-based budgeting — we just need to keep the lights on." Opportunity-based budgeting works at any scale; what changes is the number of bets, not the framing.

**Cheapest first-week move:** Take next quarter's planned spend and re-frame *one* line item as an investment thesis. Walk the CFO through it. If it lands, do it for two more next quarter.

---

### CFO-3: Whether MQL→SQL conversion is in CFO's monthly dashboard

**Diagnostic question:** Is MQL to SQL conversion in the CFO's monthly dashboard?

**Why this matters:** This is the leading indicator that connects marketing activity to revenue. If it is not on the CFO's dashboard, the CFO has no early-warning system for marketing performance — only the lagging revenue number.

**What healthy / yellow / red look like:**
- 🟢 On dashboard with target and variance.
- 🟡 Raw number only, no target.
- 🔴 Not visible to CFO.

**Common rationalization:** "MQL/SQL definitions are too noisy to be CFO-grade." Then fix the definitions before fighting for budget.

**Cheapest first-week move:** Email the CFO's analyst the 3-month trend with a 1-paragraph interpretation. If they ask follow-ups, you have your foothold.

---

### CFO-4: Whether marketing forecast variance is under 10 percent in last 4 quarters

**Diagnostic question:** Marketing forecast variance over the last 4 quarters?

**Why this matters:** Forecast accuracy is the operational equivalent of trust. A finance team that learns marketing's forecast is reliable will weight marketing input heavier in planning.

**What healthy / yellow / red look like:**
- 🟢 Under 10 percent every quarter.
- 🟡 Under 10 percent in 3 of 4.
- 🔴 Over 20 percent or chronic.

**Common rationalization:** "Marketing is inherently volatile — variance is unavoidable." Then forecast a range, not a point. Range-based forecasting that hits target counts as accurate.

**Cheapest first-week move:** Look at the last quarter's miss and write a 1-page root-cause for the CFO. Naming the cause pre-empts the CFO writing their own (less charitable) version.

---

### CFO-5: Whether CMO has co-built any model with CFO finance analyst

**Diagnostic question:** Have you co-built any model with a CFO finance analyst?

**Why this matters:** Co-build is the highest-leverage CFO trust ritual. When a finance analyst has touched the model, finance owns part of the result — and stops interpreting it as marketing advocacy.

**What healthy / yellow / red look like:**
- 🟢 Multiple co-owned models.
- 🟡 One co-built model.
- 🔴 No co-build relationship.

**Common rationalization:** "Finance is too busy to co-build — they would rather review than co-author." Then propose a 2-hour working session, not a project. Lower the activation energy.

**Cheapest first-week move:** Identify the finance analyst the CFO trusts most, ask them for 30 minutes to walk them through your most-questioned model, and offer to make one change they suggest.

---

### CFO-6: Whether marketing budget request is framed as investment thesis vs spend bucket

**Diagnostic question:** How is your marketing budget request framed?

**Why this matters:** Spend buckets ("brand, demand, ops") force the CFO to evaluate categories — which they cannot tie to revenue. Investment theses ("Bet A: $X to capture segment Y, payback in Z months, kill criteria K") give the CFO the language they already speak.

**What healthy / yellow / red look like:**
- 🟢 Investment thesis per bet with payback + kill criteria.
- 🟡 Investment thesis without quantified payback.
- 🔴 Spend buckets or single line item.

**Common rationalization:** "Bucket budgeting is what finance asks for." Finance asks for buckets because that is what marketing has historically given them. They will accept thesis format if you arrive with one.

**Cheapest first-week move:** Pick the single largest line item in next quarter's plan and rewrite it as a one-page thesis. Walk the CFO through it before the budget meeting.

## Section 4 — Reading the Trio Scorecard

### How to interpret total score (0-30)

The score is a normalized aggregate. It is intentionally a coarse signal — the *band* matters more than the exact number.

### Bands

- **24-30: Healthy 🟢**
  - Trio is structurally aligned. The audit is preventative maintenance.
  - Action: maintain rituals, watch the 1-2 yellow items, re-audit in 90 days.
  - Risk: complacency. Healthy trios degrade fast under leadership transitions.

- **17-23: Watch 🟡**
  - Workable but exposed. 1-2 critical gaps will compound.
  - Action: pick the highest-weight red, install one ritual to close it, re-audit in 60 days.
  - Risk: slow erosion. Watch-band CMOs often slide to at-risk within 2 quarters without intervention.

- **10-16: At-risk 🟠**
  - The CMO is operating with structural disadvantage. Budget defense will be hard.
  - Action: 30-day intervention. Pick top 3 reds, ship a visible artifact for each, install 2 recurring rituals.
  - Risk: visible budget cuts, marketing being recategorized as execution-only.

- **0-9: Crisis 🔴**
  - Trust is structurally broken. CMO tenure is at risk.
  - Action: 14-day intervention. Pick top 1 gap, focus all energy on closing it. Then move to next.
  - Risk: forced transition or role redefinition.

## Section 5 — Common Remediation Patterns

### "The weekly intelligence brief" pattern (fixes ~30 percent of CEO gaps)

**What it is:** A 1-page Monday-morning email to the CEO with: 3 customer signals, 1 competitive signal, 1 macro signal, and the marketing implication of each.

**Why it works:** It gives the CEO marketing-derived intelligence they cannot get elsewhere, on their schedule. After 6-8 weeks, the CEO starts forwarding it to the board.

**Closes:** CEO-1 (1:1 cadence supplement), CEO-2 (metric internalization), CEO-6 (CEO public quotes).

**Pitfall:** Do not turn it into a marketing status report. It is intelligence, not activity.

---

### "Co-build the attribution model with CFO finance analyst" pattern (fixes ~40 percent of CFO gaps)

**What it is:** Identify the finance analyst the CFO trusts. Spend 2 sessions walking them through your attribution model — and let them rebuild one assumption.

**Why it works:** Once the model has finance fingerprints on it, the CFO stops interpreting outputs as marketing advocacy.

**Closes:** CFO-1 (attribution trust), CFO-3 (dashboard inclusion), CFO-5 (co-build).

**Pitfall:** Do not protect the model from change. The point is shared ownership, not vindication.

---

### "Investment thesis budget format" pattern (fixes ~25 percent of budget gaps)

**What it is:** Replace bucket budgets ("brand, demand, ops") with per-bet investment theses (size, expected return, payback, kill criteria).

**Why it works:** It speaks the language the CFO already uses for capital allocation. Budget conversations stop being defensive.

**Closes:** CFO-2 (allocation basis), CFO-6 (request framing), and indirectly CFO-1 (forces clarity on ROI).

**Pitfall:** Do not over-engineer the first version. Three theses for the top three bets is enough.

---

### "Quarterly trio review" pattern (covers ~60 percent of remaining gaps)

**What it is:** A recurring 45-minute CMO/CEO/CFO review with a fixed agenda (see `templates/quarterly-trio-review.md`).

**Why it works:** Forces quarterly co-ownership of the marketing scoreboard. Surfaces drift before it becomes a crisis.

**Closes:** CEO-3 (exec sync), CEO-4 (pivot consultation), CFO-4 (forecast accuracy), and is the structural backbone for sustaining the other patterns.

**Pitfall:** If the meeting becomes a status update, it dies. The agenda must force decisions.

## Section 6 — When the Audit Is Wrong

### Self-assessment bias: CMOs over-score themselves

The most common failure mode: a CMO who scores themselves 22/30 when an honest scoring would put them at 14/30. The bias is structural — CMOs read their own intent (e.g., "the CEO and I have a great working relationship") instead of the structural evidence (e.g., "we have not had a 1:1 in 6 weeks and they did not loop me into the pivot").

### How to validate

Send the same indicator questions to the CEO and CFO blind, asking *them* to score the alignment. Then compare. Gaps of 1-2 points per indicator are normal. Gaps of 3+ points indicate the CMO's self-perception is materially wrong.

The absolute gold standard: anonymized 360-style feedback from 3-5 cross-functional peers (CRO, CPO, COO, head of finance, head of strategy). This surfaces blind spots the CEO/CFO will not say directly.

### Red flags that the score is too charitable

- The CMO scored every indicator at "a" or "b" — uniformly green is almost never true.
- The CMO interpreted "consulted on the pivot" as "I was in the room when it was announced."
- The CMO scored CFO trust as healthy but the CFO has never publicly defended the attribution model.
- The CMO has not had a 1:1 with the CFO in 60+ days but scored CFO axis green.
- The CMO scored "CEO can articulate metrics" as green but has never tested it.

If 2 or more red flags apply, re-run the audit with the CEO/CFO answering the same questions. The delta is the real audit.
