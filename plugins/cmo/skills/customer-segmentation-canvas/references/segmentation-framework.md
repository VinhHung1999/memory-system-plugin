# Segmentation Framework — IE Methodology Reference

This is the comprehensive reference for the customer-segmentation-canvas skill. Load it on demand when you need the full reasoning chain behind a recommendation, or when you need to brief a stakeholder on the IE methodology end-to-end.

Built from IE Business School Positioning course (Module 2), with expert input from Jaime Veiga (Johnson & Johnson, formerly Procter & Gamble — global strategic insights).

---

## Section 1 — The Why-Segment Argument

### Markets fragment over time

The clearest evidence is the music industry timeline:

- **1950s** — rock and roll. One dominant cluster.
- **1960s** — rhythm and blues, folk. Two clusters.
- **1970s** — funk, glam, rock. Three clusters.
- **1980s** — dance, Brit pop, new wave. Three more.
- **1990s** — grunge, punk, dance, house. Four clusters in one decade.

The trend is monotonic: more clusters, smaller clusters, faster turnover. The same fragmentation happened in books, food, fashion, software, and almost every B2B category. Today's customer is not the 1950s customer. The 1950s customer was naive, homogeneous, function-driven, and accepted opaque pricing and long product lifecycles. Today's customer is demanding, emotion-driven, expects transparent pricing, and tolerates short lifecycles.

If your segmentation logic still assumes the 1950s customer, you are competing for a customer who no longer exists.

### What segmentation enables

A real segmentation lets you do four things you cannot do otherwise:

1. **Differentiation** — you can build a value proposition that genuinely fits one cluster instead of an averaged-down compromise that fits no one.
2. **Premium pricing** — buyers pay a premium for a product that fits their cluster precisely. Look at the salt aisle: Kosher, Maldon, Celtic, Himalayan all command 3-10x the unit price of generic table salt.
3. **Acquisition efficiency** — narrower targeting drives higher conversion rates, lower CAC, and shorter sales cycles. You stop spending against people who would never buy.
4. **Loyalty** — customers who feel served stay. Customers who feel ignored churn.

### The cost of NOT segmenting

If you do not segment when the market has fragmented, you converge on three predictable failure modes:

- **Race-to-bottom price competition** — without differentiation, the only lever left is price.
- **Undifferentiated brand** — buyers cannot articulate what you stand for. Your brand survey scores hover around the category average.
- **Low conversion** — your funnel leaks because you are speaking to mismatched audiences. Lead-to-opportunity rates collapse.

### When NOT to segment

Segmentation is not free. It introduces additional production cost, additional commercialization cost, separate branding, separate labeling, separate go-to-market motions. There are categories where these costs do not pay back: generic table salt is the canonical example — buyers spend zero seconds deciding which brand to grab. Skip segmentation in categories with truly homogeneous preferences and zero decision time.

The decision rule: segment when the differentiation premium exceeds the per-segment incremental cost. Otherwise, run a mass strategy and compete on unit-cost economics.

## Section 2 — The 4 Segmentation Levels

The four levels form a continuum from broadest to narrowest:

```
mass market  ──  market segments  ──  niche markets  ──  segments-of-one
   broad   <--------------------------------------->     narrow
```

### Mass market

Treat the entire market as one. Same product, same message, same price for everybody. Ford Model T is the textbook example: one model, one color, one feature set. Mass marketing makes sense when:

- Preferences are genuinely homogeneous across the population.
- Economies of scale dominate — unit cost falls fast with volume.
- Variability across customers is small relative to the cost of customization.

Mass marketing is rare today and rarer in B2B. CPG basics (table salt, sugar, certain commodities) still defend it. Most other categories have fragmented past the point where mass works.

### Market segments

Identify pools of customers who share characteristics, needs, or behaviors, and design a value proposition tailored to each. This is the most common reality in B2B and B2C today. A segment is "a pool of customers that shares similar characteristics or behaves in a similar way" — emphasis on the word *similar*. Members of a segment are not identical; they are close enough that one value proposition fits them all.

### Niche markets

A niche is a narrow cluster within a segment, defined by additional shared traits. The course's example: rock-band members over 35 living in a specific area. Niches let you charge a premium because nobody else fits the cluster precisely. Early-stage companies almost always start in a niche — focus is the cardinal virtue when resources are scarce.

### Segments of one (individuals)

The narrowest level: serve one named customer with one customized value proposition. Common in B2B enterprise sales (each Fortune 500 account gets bespoke handling), in luxury (a private banker tailoring a portfolio), and in personalization-driven categories (Netflix recommendations are a soft form of segment-of-one).

Mass customization is the related pattern: define a core product that is identical across customers, and let customers self-customize via a fixed set of add-ons at a premium price. Dell PCs and Nike iD are textbook examples. The economics are clever — you preserve manufacturing scale on the core while charging a premium for the toppings.

### Decision tree: which level for which company stage

| Stage | Recommended level | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-seed / Seed | Niche | One cluster, deep know-how, premium pricing, focus |
| Series A | Niche or single market segment | Same; resist expansion until repeatability is proven |
| Series B | 1-2 market segments | Beachhead is proven; expand into adjacent segments selectively |
| Series C+ | 2-4 market segments or full coverage | Scale economics may justify breadth; B2B usually stays multi-segment, not full coverage |
| Mature / public | Re-segment every 4-5 years (J&J cadence) | Markets fragment; segments drift |

**Anti-pattern**: an early-stage company chasing "full market coverage" before product-market fit is a near-certain failure mode. Focus first.

## Section 3 — The 4 Criteria Types

Once you have picked a level, you need to pick criteria for forming clusters. There are four families, and they are complementary, not exclusive.

### Geographic

Country, region, urban/rural, climate, postal code. Use when location drives need difference:

- Mild salsa for Spain vs. spicy salsa for Mexico — climate and culture both matter.
- Heating vs. cooling product mix differs by climate zone.
- City-level positioning differs by neighborhood income and culture.

Limitation: geography is easy to capture but rarely the deepest driver of need. Use it as an overlay, not the primary criterion.

### Demographic

Gender, age, income, family size, education, occupation. Use when life-stage drives need:

- Razors and perfumes split cleanly by gender.
- Trolleys for kids, services for elderly — age + family size matter.
- Luxury hotels, cultural magazines — income + education matter.

**Demographic trap**: demographics tempt you because they are easy to measure. They are also full of false stereotypes — "old people don't play video games" is wrong, "young people don't care about retirement" is wrong, "mid-market controllers all want the same thing" is wrong. Use demographic criteria as an overlay for sizing and channel access, never as the sole basis. A demographic-only segment will fail the Differentiable filter — customers who look alike often DON'T need different things.

### Psychographic

Values, attitudes, lifestyle, personality, aspirations. Use when belief drives behavior:

- Formula One fans cut across age, gender, income.
- Innovators vs. laggards (Rogers' diffusion curve) cut across demographics.
- Extroverted vs. introverted, environmentalist vs. utilitarian — psychographic clusters explain divergent behavior in the same demographic group.

P&G runs cross-category segmentation on need-states, which is psychographic + behavioral. They commit to that lens consistently across categories so the organization develops a shared vocabulary.

### Behavioral

What customers DO — usage rate, brand loyalty, benefits sought, occasion of use, role in the decision-making process. Use when behavior is observable and predicts future action:

- Honeymoon vs. business-trip vs. holiday hotel guests need different offerings.
- Loyal vs. occasional vs. never-tried-the-brand customers respond to different marketing-mix.
- Decision-maker vs. influencer vs. user have different evaluation criteria in B2B sales.

Behavioral data is increasingly available thanks to barcodes, loyalty cards, CRM, web analytics, and digital marketing. The trend in FMCG and B2B SaaS is to lean more on behavioral because the data is rich.

### When to use each: combined

The criteria are not exclusive. Profile each candidate cluster on all four dimensions in parallel:

- **Demographic**: who they are (firmographic for B2B — industry, size, geo, role).
- **Geographic**: where they are (often subsumed into demographic in B2B).
- **Psychographic**: what they value, what they aspire to, what they fear.
- **Behavioral**: what they do today and what they have done in the past.

The IE recommendation: **start with behavioral**, layer **psychographic** to explain it, use **demographic + geographic only as overlays**.

## Section 4 — The 5-Filter MAPDA Validator

Every candidate segment must pass all five filters. If it fails one, revise. If it fails two or more, replace.

### M — Measurable

**Definition**: you can size the segment (count of customers, total addressable revenue) and characterize it from data.

**How to test**: source a count from a public or commercial dataset — government statistics (e.g., National Institute of Statistics in Spain), LinkedIn Sales Nav, Crunchbase, ZoomInfo, BuiltWith, Apollo, industry reports.

**Common failure modes**:
- "Many companies match this profile" — vague, not a number.
- "Mid-market companies" — not a count, not a source.
- "Forward-thinking buyers" — unmeasurable by definition.

**Cheapest verification step**: a 30-minute LinkedIn Sales Nav firmographic filter run with the segment criteria. If you cannot produce a count in 30 minutes, the segment is not Measurable.

### A — Accessible

**Definition**: you can reach the segment through identifiable channels — marketplaces, partner networks, communities, conferences, publications, paid channels with the right targeting.

**How to test**: name 2-3 specific channels per segment. Not "marketing campaigns" or "digital marketing" — those are categories, not channels. Specifically: which marketplace, which partner, which community, which conference, which publication.

**Common failure modes**:
- "Reach via marketing campaigns" — not a channel.
- "Online" — not a channel.
- Aspirational psychographic targeting with no actual channel that filters for the trait.

**Cheapest verification step**: try to buy or earn one ad impression / one inbound lead / one event sponsorship in your named channel. If you cannot, you cannot reach this segment.

### P — Profitable

**Definition**: ARPC × size × penetration × LTV justifies the GTM motion. The course makes clear there are two viable shapes: large segment with low margins (volume play), or small segment with high margins (premium play). Both must yield a sound total profit.

**How to test**: build a simple TAM-SAM-SOM model:
- TAM = full segment count × full ARPC range.
- SAM = subset realistically reachable through your channels.
- SOM (Year 1) = SAM × realistic penetration (often 1-3%).
- Compare SOM to GTM cost. If SOM under $1M, the segment is too small.

**Common failure modes**:
- ARPC range absent — cannot model.
- Size absent — cannot model.
- Penetration assumption magical (e.g., "we'll get 30%" without evidence).
- Costs ignored — selling to enterprise looks profitable until you price the sales motion.

**Cheapest verification step**: 1-page TAM-SAM-SOM sheet. If the math does not work on a napkin, it will not work in production.

### D — Differentiable

**Definition**: this segment responds DIFFERENTLY to marketing-mix changes than adjacent segments. Their needs, JTBDs, and buying triggers diverge.

**How to test**: 5-7 customer interviews per candidate segment, comparing answers across segments. If the JTBDs converge, the segments are not differentiable — collapse them.

**Common failure modes**:
- Demographic-only segments — customers who look alike but don't need different things.
- Aspirational segments — every buyer self-identifies as "forward-thinking" so the trait does not split anyone.
- Vanity segments — flattering to the buyer but operationally identical to neighbors.

**Cheapest verification step**: 7 customer interviews. Ask the same JTBD questions across segments. If you cannot tell the transcripts apart, the segments are not differentiable.

### A — Actionable

**Definition**: your organization can serve this segment with current product, sales motion, and CS motion — or with a clearly-bounded incremental investment.

**How to test**: rate capability fit on three axes — product, sales, CS. If all three are high, accept. If any is low, the segment requires a strategic build, not a marketing decision.

**Common failure modes**:
- Segment requires features the product does not have and will not have for 6+ months.
- Segment requires a sales motion (e.g., field enterprise) the company does not run.
- Segment requires a CS playbook (e.g., white-glove implementation) the team has not built.

**Cheapest verification step**: ask sales, product, and CS leaders independently: "Could we close, ship, and renew a customer in this segment in 90 days?" If any one says no, the segment is not currently Actionable.

## Section 5 — Kotler's 5 Segmentation Strategies

Once you have validated 1-N segments, you choose a strategy for which to serve.

### 1. Single-segment concentration

Focus all resources on ONE segment. Build deep know-how, command pricing power, earn category leadership. Examples:

- Tesla early years — luxury early-adopter EV buyers, nothing else.
- Red Bull at launch — extreme-sports enthusiasts.
- Lotus engineering — sports-car enthusiasts.

**When to choose**: early-stage companies. Niche-led market entry. Category creation.
**Risk**: severe exposure to demand shifts in the chosen segment. Mitigate with adjacency-mapping every 6 months.

### 2. Selective specialization

Serve multiple unrelated segments with different products. Each segment must be profitable on its own — no cross-subsidy. Examples:

- Mercedes E-class, A-class, and Smart for different segments.
- A holding company running unrelated category brands.

**When to choose**: when you can credibly serve segments that don't share much, and each one stands alone economically.
**Risk**: organizational complexity. Brand-portfolio governance. Synergies are weak so unit economics must be strong per segment.

### 3. Market specialization

Focus on ONE customer segment and serve them with multiple products covering different needs. Examples:

- Specializing in elderly care with a portfolio of products (mobility, nutrition, finance, social).
- Specializing in remote-first SaaS companies with a portfolio (HR, security, ops, analytics).

**When to choose**: when one customer type dominates and their needs are deep enough to support a multi-product roadmap.
**Risk**: concentration risk on the customer type. If that segment slows, the whole portfolio slows.

### 4. Product specialization

ONE product, adapted to the specific requirements of each segment. Examples:

- A piano manufacturer offering kids' models, band models, and professional models — same instrument, three configurations.
- A SaaS platform with industry editions (healthcare edition, finance edition, education edition).

**When to choose**: when product mastery is the moat and segments mostly differ on configuration, not on product class.
**Risk**: if product class becomes commoditized, all segments lose at once.

### 5. Full market coverage

Serve every segment. Two flavors:

- **Differentiated** — different products / brands per segment. L'Oreal is the textbook example: L'Oreal Paris and Maybelline for mass, Helena Rubinstein and Biotherm for luxury, Kerastase for professional, La Roche-Posay for active. House-of-brands strategy.
- **Undifferentiated** — one product for everyone (mass marketing). Generic table salt.

**When to choose**: late-stage companies with deep balance sheets, strong brand portfolios, and scale economics that justify the breadth.
**Risk**: operational complexity, brand cannibalization, ICP drift across segments.

### Choosing rule of thumb

| Stage | Default strategy |
|---|---|
| Early | Single-segment concentration |
| Mid | Selective specialization (1-2 segments) or single-segment concentration |
| Late B2B | Market specialization or selective specialization |
| Late B2C with scale economics | Full market coverage (differentiated, house-of-brands) |
| Late B2C with homogeneous demand | Full market coverage (undifferentiated, mass) |

Re-review every 4-5 years per Jaime Veiga's J&J cadence. Markets fragment; segments drift; competitors enter.

## Section 6 — From Segments to Positioning

The IE sequence is fixed:

```
5Cs analysis  →  Segment  →  TARGET (pick 1+ segment)  →  Position  →  4Ps
```

Segmentation is the input to targeting. Targeting is the input to positioning. Positioning is the input to the 4Ps (product, price, place, promotion).

### How segmentation feeds positioning

For each chosen target segment:

- The **frame of reference** in the positioning statement is scoped per segment. ("For mid-market controllers" is a different frame from "For enterprise CFOs.")
- The **point of difference** is whatever wins inside that segment's competitive set.
- The **reason to believe** must be evidence the segment trusts (not a generic proof point).
- The **value proposition** is tuned to the segment's JTBD.

If you skip segmentation and go straight to positioning, you end up with a generic positioning statement that fits no one precisely. The classic failure mode.

### Common error: positioning before segmenting

Reverses cause and effect. The positioning statement says "we help X do Y better than Z because W." If X is undefined or under-defined (a non-segment), the rest of the statement collapses. Do segmentation first; do positioning second.

### Anti-pattern: re-segmenting to match an existing positioning

Sometimes a marketing leader inherits a positioning statement and tries to construct a segmentation that justifies it retrospectively. This is also wrong — the segmentation must be honest about who the customer actually is, even if that contradicts current positioning. If they conflict, fix one or the other; do not paper over.

## Section 7 — Expert Insights (Jaime Veiga, J&J / formerly P&G)

Jaime Veiga leads global insight projects at Johnson & Johnson and previously led many segmentation projects at Procter & Gamble. His high-leverage insights:

### Two golden questions BEFORE research

Before you commission segmentation research, answer:

1. **Who is the real consumer for my brand?**
2. **Who do I want to target?**

If you cannot answer these in a paragraph each, the research will be wasted. Segmentation research is downstream of strategic intent.

### Anchor on jobs-to-be-done, not features

The deepest segmentation research is done on **need-states** (psychographic + behavioral). P&G commits to need-state segmentation across categories so the organization develops a shared vocabulary. Need-states are jobs-to-be-done framed as customer states of mind.

Feature-based segments ("buyers who want feature X") are shallow because features change. JTBD-based segments ("buyers solving for the morning energy boost") are durable because the job is durable.

### Validate via primary research

Quantitative segmentation surveys are necessary but the analysis is where the value lives. Most companies underinvest in analysis and overinvest in survey design. Inverse the ratio.

For B2B and growth-stage companies: **5-7 customer interviews per candidate segment minimum** before committing GTM budget. If the JTBDs converge across segments, collapse them. If you cannot recruit 5-7 customers in the segment, the segment is too small or too inaccessible.

### Operationalize results

After analysis, market researchers must behave like marketers:

- Give each segment a **memorable name**. ("Forward-thinking SaaS leaders" is a vanity name; "Multi-currency mid-market controllers" is an operational name.)
- **Present them so the whole org recognizes them** — slides, posters, internal wiki, leadership offsites. The segment names should be a working vocabulary across product, sales, CS, and finance.
- **Link them to concept testing, pricing research, and R&D testing** so all downstream research is segmentation-aware.

### Cadence

Re-segment every **4-5 years**. Yearly re-segmentation is wasteful and creates organizational whiplash. But never re-segmenting is also a mistake — markets drift. Trigger an off-cycle re-segmentation when results materially miss plan and the existing segmentation cannot explain why.

### Examples of successful re-segmentation

- **Cheetos in Europe**: shifted target from kids to teenagers because consumer behavior had shifted teenager-oriented.
- **Herbal Essences**: shifted from organic-herbs / natural-ingredients positioning to experiential / modern positioning. Whole new segmentation at global level.
- **Apple**: expanded beyond top-standard early-adopter target to also serve value-oriented buyers via previous-generation models.

Each was a deliberate, expensive, strategic re-segmentation — not a tweak.

## Section 8 — Common Segmentation Failure Modes

Catalog of failures to recognize and reject:

### 1. Demographic-only segments

"Mid-market companies, 50-500 employees" is a demographic profile, not a segment. Customers in this profile do not share need-similarity by virtue of being mid-market. Demographic-only segments fail the Differentiable filter.

**Fix**: layer behavioral + psychographic. "Mid-market companies running NetSuite who reconcile multi-currency manually" is a real segment because behavior + need diverge from neighbors.

### 2. Aspirational segments without channel access

"Forward-thinking SaaS leaders who value innovation and embrace new technology" is aspirational. Every SaaS leader self-identifies this way. Even if it were real, there is no channel that filters for the trait. Fails Accessible.

**Fix**: re-anchor on observable behavior. "CTOs at $50M+ SaaS companies who are evaluating their data warehouse in the next 12 months" is testable: you can find them, you can size them, you can reach them through dbt community or Snowflake summit.

### 3. Vanity segments

Segments that flatter the buyer ("forward-thinking", "premium", "discerning", "sophisticated") but cannot be reached. The buyer self-identifies in the trait, but the trait is not a real signal. Fails Differentiable + Accessible.

**Fix**: replace vanity language with role + behavior + situation.

### 4. Segments too small for Profitable

A genuinely tight niche may be too small for the GTM motion to pay back. SOM under $1M means the cost of building product + sales + CS for the segment exceeds the return. Fails Profitable.

**Fix**: either broaden the segment definition, raise ARPC, or pick a different segment.

### 5. Skipping psychographic + behavioral, going straight to demo

The fastest, laziest, most common failure mode. Demographics are easy to source — LinkedIn firmographics, ZoomInfo filters, government statistics. So teams build demographic profiles and call them segments. But demographic profiles do not predict need-similarity. Fails Differentiable.

**Fix**: invest in primary research. Interview customers. Cluster on JTBDs. Then overlay demographics for sizing and channel access.

### 6. Segments that do not align with corporate strategy

A premium-brand strategy needs a high-end-customer segmentation. A volume strategy needs a broad-and-shallow segmentation. If segmentation contradicts corporate strategy, both will fail. Fails the meta-test in section 2.7 of the IE course.

**Fix**: align segmentation with corporate strategy explicitly. If strategy is changing, re-segment. If segmentation is changing, brief the C-suite first.

### 7. Static segmentation — never updated

Segmentation done once and never revisited. The world drifts; the customer drifts; the segmentation does not. Eventually the operating model is segmentation-blind. Fails the Veiga 4-5-year cadence test.

**Fix**: schedule a re-segmentation review every 4-5 years, or off-cycle when results materially miss plan.

### 8. Segments designed by committee

A 10-segment scheme that gives every internal stakeholder a constituency is not a segmentation — it is a political settlement. Real segmentations are 2-5 segments, ruthlessly chosen, with explicit ANTI-segments named. If your segmentation is "everyone matters", you have not segmented.

**Fix**: name 2-5 segments. Name the anti-segments out loud. Resist additions unless they pass MAPDA.
