# IE Business School 4-Part Marketing Plan Framework

A complete reference for the IE Business School marketing-plan methodology, distilled from the 41-lecture Marketing Plan course (Course 4 of the Marketing Strategy Specialization), with expert insights from Veronica Santos and Luis Baptista.

---

## Section 1 — The IE 4-Part Structure (Veronica Santos rule)

### Strict order: Analysis → Strategy → Mix → Results

A real marketing plan answers FOUR questions, IN ORDER:

| Part | Question | Section name | What it produces |
|------|----------|--------------|------------------|
| 1 | Where are we? | ANALYSIS | 5Cs + Internal differentiation/lifecycle + SWOT 2x2 |
| 2 | Where do we want to be? | STRATEGY | Quantified objectives + target segment + positioning |
| 3 | How do we get there? | MARKETING MIX | Product / Price / Channel / Communication 4Ps |
| 4 | How do we measure? | EXPECTED RESULTS | Metrics mirroring Part 2 + budget + anti-plan + kill criteria |

### Each part answers ONE question

Do not let a part answer a question from a different part. The most common failure is a "plan" that opens with tactics ("we'll run a paid social campaign in Q3") — that's a Part 3 fragment with no Part 1 evidence and no Part 2 strategy. It cannot survive a CFO challenge because the structural reasoning is missing.

### Why reordering breaks the chain

Each part is the INPUT to the next:
- Part 1 SWOT → Part 2 objectives (you can't set targets without knowing the market)
- Part 2 strategy → Part 3 mix (you can't pick channels without knowing the target)
- Part 2 objectives → Part 4 metrics (you can't measure what you didn't commit to)

If you reorder — most commonly Strategy-before-Analysis — the inputs are missing. You end up justifying yesterday's decision with tomorrow's data, which is how organizations end up "validating" plans that should never have been written.

**Veronica Santos rule:** the 4-part order is non-negotiable. If a draft does not have all 4 parts in this order, it is not a marketing plan; it is a deck.

## Section 2 — Part 1: Analysis (Where are we?)

The analysis is split into TWO halves: external + internal. Each looks for something specific.

### External analysis — 5Cs framework

| C | What you analyze | Output |
|---|------------------|--------|
| Context | PEST: Political / Economic / Social / Technological | Macro forces shaping the market |
| Customers | Needs, segments, buying behavior, TAM | Where the demand is and how it buys |
| Collaborators | Partners, distributors, integrators | Dependency map + leverage points |
| Competitors | Direct + indirect — full marketing strategy of each | Positioning map + white space |
| Company | How the market sees us today | Reputation gaps |

### Market analysis — 4 dimensions, with NUMBERS

For the market itself:
1. **Size** — units, value, number of individuals (e.g., "4 million cars/yr in Germany")
2. **Trend** — growing / stable / declining (with rate)
3. **Competitive rivalry** — number of competitors + how they compete
4. **Attractiveness** — overall pull (margins, growth, accessibility)

A vibe doesn't count. Pin a number to each dimension.

### Competitor analysis (reverse-engineer the marketing strategy)

For every direct competitor, fill in:
- Target segment they pursue
- Positioning they own
- Marketing mix: product config, pricing, channel, communication

Don't stop at "they sell electric cars." That's not analysis.

### Customer segmentation — industry-specific lenses

Combine multiple dimensions: demographic + psychographic + behavioral + lifecycle stage. Single-dimension segmentation is unsophisticated.

A segment = group of customers with **common need AND common buying behavior**. If either is missing, it's not a real segment.

### Internal analysis — exactly 2 deliverables (not more)

The internal half delivers exactly TWO things:

1. **Elements of differentiation** (from CUSTOMER perspective only)
   - List expansively first (4-6 items)
   - Pick one for positioning later in Part 2
   - Customer view only — if the customer wouldn't say "this is better," strike it
   - Example MOOC differentiators: cheaper, no academic prerequisites, time-flexible, latest technology

2. **Stage in the product life cycle**
   - Introduction / Growth / Maturity / Decline
   - Place the product on the curve based on sales + profit shape
   - This drives objective-setting AND mix tactics
   - Launch plan ≠ maturity-defense plan; they use opposite KPIs

### SWOT 2x2 — the summary, not the brainstorm

| | INTERNAL | EXTERNAL |
|---|---|---|
| **Positive** | **Strengths** | **Opportunities** |
| **Negative** | **Weaknesses** | **Threats** |

Strict rules:
- Internal column comes ONLY from internal analysis (differentiation + lifecycle)
- External column comes ONLY from external scan (market / customers / competitors)
- NEVER mix rows. A "weakness" in the wrong cell is a category error.
- 3-5 specific items per cell minimum
- SWOT is a SUMMARY at the end of analysis, not a brainstorming tool — every item must be a defensible conclusion from prior data
- Make weaknesses + threats brutally honest (e.g., "no Steve Jobs anymore," "lack of recent innovation"). Generic SWOTs don't drive decisions.

### Action verbs for downstream

In the marketing plan that follows, every decision must:
- LEVERAGE a strength
- EXPLOIT an opportunity
- HIDE / mitigate a weakness
- DEFEND against a threat

Each Part 2/3/4 line item should map to one of these 4 verbs.

### Nissan Leaf launch case — 2 fixed constraints + 4 decision blocks

The Nissan Leaf launch in Germany establishes the constraint-header pattern:
- **Constraint 1:** Sales target = 2,000 units year 1
- **Constraint 2:** Budget = €2.5M

These are written at the top of every section as guardrails. They prevent scope creep mid-plan.

Then the 4 decision blocks follow:
1. Product configuration (car + battery bundled vs leased separately)
2. Pricing (€30K headline, modeled across subsidy stack scenarios)
3. Distribution (filter the 600 dealers — only urban dealers fit a city EV)
4. Communication (TV / digital / social mix anchored to budget)

Lessons that generalize:
- For radical innovation in a hostile home-bias market, pick a niche where nationality doesn't matter (eco-conscious, urban early-adopter)
- Product configuration is a strategic lever, not just a SKU choice
- Channel breadth must match segment density — concentrate, don't sprawl
- Pricing must be modeled in 2-3 net-price scenarios before locking communication

## Section 3 — Part 2: Strategy (Where to go?)

Strategy is the second part and the most strategic. It has 5 sub-sections, in this exact order:

### 5 sections required, in order

1. **Financial objectives** (4 quantified rows minimum)
2. **Non-financial objectives** (brand)
3. **Customer objectives** (attraction vs retention split)
4. **Target segment** (STP)
5. **Positioning statement**

### Financial objectives — 4 rows minimum

Always quantified, always time-boxed:

| # | Row | Example |
|---|-----|---------|
| 1 | Sales — units | 2,000 cars in year 1 |
| 2 | Sales — value (revenue/ARR/GMV) | $30M ARR |
| 3 | Market share (or category share) | 5pct of EV segment |
| 4 | Profit | Gross contribution / net contribution / NOI |

Sanity check: if year-1 target exceeds 5pct of TAM for a niche product, it's likely unrealistic. Nissan's 2,000 units in a 4M-car market = 0.05pct, which is challenging-but-achievable for a radical innovation.

### Non-financial objectives — brand

Pick metrics that match the marketing actions you actually plan to run:
- Brand awareness (only if running advertising)
- Brand preference / consideration / purchase intent
- Brand associations (3 attribute dials you want to own)
- Digital signals: clicks, searches, followers, UGC, NPS — only if your channel mix includes digital

Don't list metrics you won't actually influence. That's KPI-theater.

### Customer objectives — explicit pct split

Two levers: attraction (new customers) vs retention (existing customers). The split must be EXPLICIT, not implicit, because Part 4 budget allocation depends on it.

Lifecycle drives the split:
- Introduction stage → heavy attraction (e.g., 80/20 split)
- Maturity stage → heavy retention (e.g., 30/70 split)
- Decline → defensive retention + selective attraction

Apple iPhone 6/7: most objectives were retention-weighted because the install base was massive.

### Target segment — STP, strict order

Segmentation → Targeting → Positioning, in this order, never reversed.

- **Segmentation:** apply industry-specific criteria (demographic + psychographic + behavioral)
- **Targeting:** select 1+ segments based on MAPDA test (Measurable / Accessible / Profitable / Differentiable / Actionable)
- **Positioning:** done in section 5

A target named "mid-market companies" is too broad. Run the segmentation skill first.

### Positioning statement

Use the 3-element template:

> For **[target segment]**, **[brand]** is the **[frame of reference / category]** that delivers **[unique value / point of difference]**, because **[reason to believe]**.

3 components:
- Frame of reference (competitor set we anchor against)
- Point of difference (the ONE differentiator picked from Part 1's expansive list)
- Reasons to believe (3 proof points)

### Kotler's 2-pillar minimum

Kotler's classic definition requires:
1. Target market (defined)
2. Value proposition (defined)

Both must appear explicitly. If either is missing, the strategy is incomplete and you cannot move to Part 3.

## Section 4 — Part 3: Marketing Mix (How to get there?)

The 4Ps, updated:
- **Product**
- **Price**
- **Channel** (replacing the older "Place")
- **Communication** (replacing the older "Promotion")

Each P must trace to a strategic choice in Part 2. No P can be picked in isolation.

### Product

- **Lifecycle stage** (carry from Part 1) drives feature/roadmap decisions
- **Product levels** (Kotler):
  - Core benefit (the job-to-be-done)
  - Basic product (features delivering the core)
  - Expected product (table-stakes)
  - Augmented product (support, SLA, community, docs)
  - Potential product (future expansion)
- **Branding decisions:** brand architecture, naming, sub-brands

### Price

- **Approach:** cost-based / value-based / competitive — pick + justify
- **Positioning fit:** the price tier must match the Part 2 positioning (premium, mid, penetration)
- **6-criterion checklist:**
  1. Cost floor known?
  2. Customer willingness-to-pay known?
  3. Competitor benchmark?
  4. Subsidy / discount stack modeled?
  5. Price elasticity assumption?
  6. Anchor / reference price set?

### Channel — 4-step design

1. **Customer need:** where does the target buy?
2. **Channel options:** own / partner / marketplace / direct sales / mixed
3. **Channel economics:** margin and CAC by channel
4. **Channel mix decision + cuts:** name the channels you DON'T use and why

Channel breadth must match segment density. Filter, don't sprawl.

### Communication — 5 M's + IMC + buyer-readiness

**5 M's:**
- Mission (objective)
- Message (positioning expressed in 1 sentence)
- Media (paid / owned / earned mix)
- Money (budget allocation)
- Measurement (forwards to Part 4)

**IMC 6-criteria:**
- Coverage / contribution / commonality / complementarity / conformability / cost

**Buyer-readiness mix** (allocate budget by stage):
- Awareness — mass-reach channels
- Knowledge / interest — content
- Preference / conviction — social proof, demos
- Purchase — sales enablement, retargeting

## Section 5 — Part 4: Expected Results (How to measure?)

### Mirror rule (the most important rule in Part 4)

Every Part 2 objective MUST have exactly one Part 4 metric. One-to-one. No orphans.

If Part 2 says "lift NPS by 10 points," Part 4 must have a row: NPS metric, measurement tool (brand tracker), target (+10), cadence (quarterly), owner.

Conversely: every Part 4 metric must trace back to a Part 2 objective. A metric without an objective is activity tracking, not impact measurement.

### Drucker's rule

> "What doesn't get measured doesn't get managed."

If you cannot define a measurement tool + cadence + owner for a metric, drop the underlying objective in Part 2. Don't carry zombie objectives.

### Marketing as investment, not expense (Luis Baptista insight)

Every line item in the budget should be presented as an investment with an expected return — not a cost to be minimized. The objective-and-task method (below) supports this framing because each tactic is bound to an objective.

### Budget — sum of all expenditures, computed by objective-and-task

Total budget = sum of every line item across the plan. Methods:

| Method | Verdict |
|--------|---------|
| Affordable (whatever's left) | AVOID — signals marketing is lowest priority |
| Percentage of sales | OK if calibrated by industry / lifecycle / volume |
| Competitive parity | AVOID — defers your decisions to competitors |
| **Objective-and-task** | **RECOMMENDED — links spend to objectives** |

Objective-and-task method:
1. List Part 2 objectives
2. Define tactics needed to reach each
3. Cost each tactic
4. Sum to total budget

Each line item links explicitly to a Part 2 objective. This is the only method that survives a CFO challenge.

### Anti-plan + kill criteria + decision gates

A real plan also says what we are NOT doing and what makes us stop:

- **Anti-plan:** channels / segments / tactics deliberately excluded (and why)
- **Kill criteria:** "IF leading metric X is below threshold Y by date Z, THEN stop / pivot"
- **Decision gates:** quarterly review points with named decision, owner, data needed

Without these, the plan is unfalsifiable and unfundable.

## Section 6 — The Constraint Header Rule (Nissan Leaf)

Every section's TOP must show the two fixed constraints:

> **CONSTRAINT HEADER:**
> Sales target: <X>
> Budget: $<Y>

Why? Two reasons:
1. **Scope creep prevention.** Without the header, mid-plan decisions drift. With it, every paragraph is judged against the same yardstick.
2. **Decision discipline.** Every choice must respect both constraints. If a tactic violates one, it cannot enter the plan.

This rule comes directly from the Nissan Leaf launch case: 2,000 units + €2.5M were stated at the top of every analysis section, every strategy section, every mix section, every results section. Nothing got slipped in.

## Section 7 — Common failure modes

| Failure | What it looks like | Why it fails |
|---------|-------------------|--------------|
| Plans that start with tactics | "Q3 plan: run a paid social campaign for $200K" | No Part 1, no Part 2 — tactics with no strategic anchor |
| Part 4 metrics that don't trace to Part 2 | KPI dashboard with impressions, CTR, CPL but no link to ARR or NPS | Activity metrics, not impact metrics |
| "Mid-market companies" as target | Target segment too broad to operationalize | Segmentation skipped — every plan choice becomes generic |
| Mix without budget allocation | 4Ps listed but no $ per channel | Cannot defend the plan or measure ROI |
| No anti-plan | Everything is in scope | Plan is unfalsifiable |
| No kill criteria | No threshold for stopping | Plan runs forever, even when failing |
| No decision gates | No named review points | Plan is not actually managed |
| Reordering parts | Strategy before Analysis | Inputs missing for Strategy — most common error |
| SWOT mixing rows | "Weakness: Android is strong" | Threat in the wrong cell — category error |
| 4Ps in isolation | Pricing decided without referencing positioning | Each P decoupled from strategy |
| Vague financial objectives | "Grow sales meaningfully" | Cannot be measured, defended, or used |
| Same metric repeated across objectives | NPS used for awareness AND retention AND positioning | Different objectives need different measurement tools |
| Affordable-method budgeting | "We'll spend whatever's left" | Marketing as expense, not investment |

## Section 8 — Expert insights

### Veronica Santos: 4-part order is non-negotiable

The most common error in marketing-plan submissions is reordering the parts (usually putting Strategy before Analysis, or starting with the Marketing Mix). This breaks the entire causal chain because:
- Strategy without Analysis = guessing
- Mix without Strategy = activity calendar
- Results without Mix = wishful thinking

The 4-part order is a load-bearing structural rule. Treat it the way an engineer treats foundation specs.

### Luis Baptista: marketing as investment business case

Marketing is an investment, not an expense. Every line item in the budget should be defended as an investment with an expected return. The objective-and-task budgeting method is the only one that supports this framing — because every tactic is bound to a Part 2 objective, and Part 2 objectives have Part 4 metrics, every dollar can be traced to an expected outcome.

This is also why CMOs who present plans using the IE 4-part structure tend to get budget approved more often than those who present activity calendars. Structure communicates discipline, and discipline communicates investment-grade thinking.

### Practical synthesis

A marketing plan that survives a board challenge has all of these:
- 4 parts in correct order
- Constraint header at the top of every section
- SWOT with cleanly separated internal/external rows
- Quantified financial objectives (4 rows minimum)
- Explicit attraction-vs-retention pct split
- Single named target segment with MAPDA validation
- 3-element positioning statement
- Each P traced explicitly to a Part 2 strategic choice
- Mirror table: every Part 2 objective → exactly one Part 4 metric
- Budget by objective-and-task method
- Anti-plan + kill criteria + decision gates

Anything less is a draft, not a plan.
