# Marketing Vision Rubric

A reference for the `cmo-strategic-vision-canvas` skill. Defines each
scoring axis, contrasts vision against neighbouring layers, walks
through annotated benchmarks, names the most common anti-patterns,
explains the stress-tests in operational terms, and lists the three
mission-anchor patterns to choose from.

The rubric is designed to answer one question:

> Can a Marketing Manager use this vision to decide which campaigns
> to kill and which to scale?

Anything that does not survive that question is a slogan, not a
vision.

---

## Section 1 — Axis Definitions

Each axis is scored 0-3. Total = 0-9. Verdict bands:

| Total | Verdict |
|-------|---------|
| 8-9   | Strategic vision |
| 5-7   | Worth revising   |
| 2-4   | Slogan           |
| 0-1   | Empty aspiration |

(In `--strict` mode the bar rises: 9 = Strategic, 7-8 = Worth revising,
4-6 = Slogan, 0-3 = Empty.)

### Clarity (0-3)

Does every team member understand the future state described?

- **0 — Opaque.** Pure vague language; no future state, no tangible signal.
  - "We will be the most loved brand in fintech."
  - "Excellence in everything we do."
- **1 — Partial.** Hints at a future state but ambiguous; reasonable people
  read it differently.
  - "Help businesses run smarter."
  - "Empower modern teams."
- **2 — Specific.** Names at least one concrete signal — a segment, a
  capability, or a mission target — that constrains interpretation.
  - "Accelerate the world's transition to sustainable energy."
  - "Make every storefront discoverable from any phone."
- **3 — Vivid.** Multiple specificity tokens stack so the future state
  is paintable. A new hire can sketch what success looks like.
  - "Reinvent travel by creating authentic local experiences."
  - "Make every patient's first 30 days after diagnosis less lonely."

### Alignment (0-3)

Does the vision link customer truth to business strategy and to the
company's broader mission?

- **0 — Standalone.** Aspirational with no link to customers or mission.
  - "Be #1 in our category."
  - "Win the future."
- **1 — Single anchor.** Mentions either a customer signal or a mission
  signal but not both, and not bridged to capability.
  - "Delight every customer."
  - "A more sustainable world."
- **2 — Bridged.** Customer truth + (mission anchor OR strategic
  capability). Marketing is clearly serving a real buyer reality.
  - "Make sustainable living commonplace." (mission + customer behaviour)
  - "Help mid-market HR teams onboard remote hires in their first week."
- **3 — Fully connected.** Customer truth + mission anchor + capability,
  all three present and reinforcing each other.
  - "Reinvent travel by creating authentic local experiences." (segment,
    mission, capability of curated locality)
  - "We're in business to save our home planet." (mission + identity +
    purpose-as-capability)

### Actionability (0-3)

Could a Marketing Manager use this vision to defend a kill/scale call
in front of the CFO?

- **0 — Pure poster.** Emotional language, no verb that implies a
  prioritisation move.
  - "We are passionate about excellence."
  - "Everyone deserves a great brand experience."
- **1 — Soft action.** A verb is present but generic; could fit any
  campaign.
  - "Serve our customers well."
  - "Build great relationships."
- **2 — Decision verb.** An action verb that implies what to invest in
  and what to deprioritise.
  - "Accelerate the world's transition to sustainable energy."
    (kill: fossil-fuel-adjacent partnerships; scale: clean-energy buyer
    education)
  - "Help mid-market HR teams onboard remote hires."
    (kill: enterprise-only campaigns; scale: SMB onboarding playbook)
- **3 — Decision verb + capability.** Action verb plus a named capability
  the org will build, so the budget line is obvious.
  - "Reinvent travel by creating authentic local experiences."
    (capability: a curated host marketplace; budget line: host
    onboarding + local-experience content engine)
  - "Make payroll the operating system of small-business growth."
    (capability: embedded financial workflows; budget line: API
    partnerships)

## Section 2 — Vision vs Positioning vs Slogan

These three are different layers. Confusing them is the most common
failure pattern in CMO board decks.

| Layer | Time horizon | Audience | Job to do | Example |
|-------|--------------|----------|-----------|---------|
| **Vision** | 5-10 years | Internal alignment + external story | Tells teams where to go, what to invest in, why their work matters | "Reinvent travel by creating authentic local experiences." |
| **Positioning** | 1-3 years | Buyers in a competitive market | Differentiates vs named competitors on a buyer-relevant axis | "The only HR platform built for mid-market remote-first companies." |
| **Slogan** | Campaign-level | Buyers (motivational) | Memorable trigger for a specific campaign | "Belong anywhere." |

### Three examples of confusing the layers

1. **Slogan posing as vision.** "Be the most loved brand in fintech."
   This is a feeling, not a future state. It can be a tagline for a
   brand campaign, but it cannot guide the budget. Rewrite it as a
   vision by naming who you serve and the capability you build.
2. **Positioning posing as vision.** "The fastest payment platform
   for mid-market merchants." This is a competitive positioning —
   useful in sales decks, useless as a 10-year north star. A vision
   should outlast the current competitive set.
3. **Vision posing as positioning.** "Reinvent travel by creating
   authentic local experiences" is a strategic vision. If a sales rep
   uses it on a discovery call, the prospect won't know what product
   to buy. Translate the vision into positioning ("the platform for
   booking local hosts in 220 countries") before going to market.

## Section 3 — Benchmark Visions (annotated)

Each benchmark is scored against the rubric so the LLM has concrete
anchor points when revising a weak vision.

### Airbnb (COVID rebound) — "Reinvent travel by creating authentic local experiences."

- **Clarity: 3/3.** Three specificity tokens stack — `experiences`,
  `local`, `authentic`. A new marketing hire can sketch the campaign
  set in a week.
- **Alignment: 3/3.** Customer truth (travellers want belonging, not
  hotel sameness) is bridged to a strategic capability (host
  marketplace) and an implicit mission (travel as connection, not
  consumption).
- **Actionability: 2/3.** "Reinvent" is a clear decision verb.
  Marginally short of 3/3 because the named capability is implicit
  rather than spelled out. In practice the company's 2020-2022
  budget reallocation toward hosts and away from generic-hotel
  performance marketing showed how operationally sharp the vision
  was.
- **Total: 8/9. Verdict: Strategic vision.**

### Tesla — "Accelerate the world's transition to sustainable energy."

- **Clarity: 2/3.** Mission target is concrete (sustainable energy)
  even without a named customer segment.
- **Alignment: 3/3.** Three mission-anchor tokens (`world`,
  `transition`, `sustainable energy`) — purpose is concrete, not
  inspirational fluff.
- **Actionability: 2/3.** `Accelerate` and `transition` are decision
  verbs that explain why Tesla open-sourced patents and built the
  Supercharger network — those moves serve the mission even when they
  hurt short-term margin.
- **Total: 7/9. Verdict: Worth revising for marketing-specific use.**
  This is a corporate mission. A marketing-specific vision derived
  from it would name the buyer and capability more sharply: e.g.,
  "Make every Tesla owner a salesperson by giving them content their
  network actually wants to share."

### Unilever — "Make sustainable living commonplace."

- **Clarity: 2/3.** Mission target is clear (sustainable living) and
  the verb (`make ... commonplace`) describes the future state.
- **Alignment: 2/3.** Customer behaviour (`living`) bridges to mission
  (`sustainable`). No explicit capability named.
- **Actionability: 2/3.** `Commonplace` is the operational ask —
  scale, distribution, mass-market accessibility. Unilever's brand
  portfolio decisions (sustainable-living brands grow faster than the
  rest) follow from this directly.
- **Total: 6/9. Verdict: Worth revising for marketing-specific use,
  but extremely strong as a corporate mission.**

### Patagonia — "We're in business to save our home planet."

- **Clarity: 2/3.** Identity statement that doubles as a mission
  target.
- **Alignment: 3/3.** Mission + purpose + identity bridge — no
  separation between what the company does commercially and what it
  exists to do.
- **Actionability: 2/3.** `Save` is the strongest possible decision
  verb in this domain. Patagonia uses it to defend buying back used
  product, suing the federal government, and donating profits — all
  decisions a normal company would never make.
- **Total: 7/9. Verdict: Worth revising for marketing-specific use,
  perfect as company mission.**

## Section 4 — Anti-patterns (annotated)

### "Be the #1 brand in our category."

- **Why it fails:** Ranking-aspiration. Tells nobody what to do.
  Three different marketing managers will interpret "#1" three
  different ways (most ARR? most social mentions? most G2 reviews?
  most Net Promoter Score?). The rubric scores it 0/9 because rank
  claims with no action verb deduct on every axis.
- **Fix recipe:** Replace the ranking claim with a customer-truth
  statement and a capability. "Become the default checkout
  experience for sub-$10M e-commerce sellers" beats "Be the #1
  payments brand."

### "Delight every customer."

- **Why it fails:** No specificity, no priority guide. "Delight" is
  a vague poster word that triggers the Clarity deduction. "Every
  customer" prevents segment focus, which kills Alignment because
  marketing cannot serve everyone equally well. Scores 2/9.
- **Fix recipe:** Pick ONE segment whose delight you'll measure.
  "Make every first-time freelance Stripe seller's first payout
  feel magical" beats "delight every customer" — even if it sounds
  smaller, it lets the team kill irrelevant work.

### "Lead through innovation."

- **Why it fails:** Generic, no anchor, applicable to any company in
  any industry. Both `innovation` and `lead` are flagged as vague
  poster words. Scores 0/9.
- **Fix recipe:** Name what you're innovating, for whom, and what
  capability that requires. "Build the first AI co-pilot for
  primary-care physicians' note-taking" tells the team what to
  invest in.

## Section 5 — Stress-tests (operational guide)

### "Managers in the room" check

**How to actually run it:**

1. Get 5 line marketing managers in a room (or a Slack channel).
2. Show them the vision text — nothing else, no context.
3. Ask each independently to write down: "What does this vision tell
   you to do this quarter? What does it tell you to stop doing?
   What's the one capability we should hire for?"
4. Compare answers. If 4 of 5 converge on roughly the same answer,
   the vision passes. If you get 5 different answers (or worse, 5
   shrugs), it's a slogan.

The script approximates this with a specificity proxy: more
customer/capability/action tokens → higher score. Real-room test is
still the gold standard.

### "Compass-vs-poster" check

A vision is a **compass** if it tells teams (a) where to go AND (b)
why their work matters. Anything that fails either side is a
**poster** — it can hang on the wall but can't navigate.

**Decision tree:**

```
Does the vision contain at least one action verb OR mission anchor?
  NO  → poster (no direction). Rewrite from scratch.
  YES → does the vision contain a customer truth OR a named capability?
          NO  → half-compass. Add the "why" by naming a segment or capability.
          YES → compass. Proceed to the budget-defence test.
```

### "Budget defence" test

The hardest stress-test, run mentally. Imagine the CFO asks: *"Why
this vision and not a different one? Where would I expect the next
\$10M of marketing budget to go because of this vision, and where
would I expect cuts?"*

- If you can name a specific budget line that grows AND a specific
  one that shrinks, the vision passes the budget-defence test.
- If you can only describe vibes ("more brand", "stronger awareness"),
  the vision is a slogan dressed up as strategy.

A real-world Airbnb example: the vision implied scaling host
onboarding content + cutting performance marketing on generic-hotel
keywords. Both are dollar decisions you can point to.

## Section 6 — Mission Anchor patterns

A marketing vision is strongest when anchored to a broader purpose.
Three patterns work; pick the one that fits your company.

### Pattern 1 — Cause-anchored

The vision is anchored to a societal cause the company exists to
serve. Marketing's job is to make the cause feel personally relevant
to the buyer.

- **When to use:** the company is mission-driven by default
  (Patagonia, Unilever, Tesla, Allbirds) or the buyer self-selects on
  values.
- **Risk:** ringfences you to buyers who care about the cause; if
  the cause goes mainstream, competitors copy the language and
  dilute it.
- **Example:** Unilever's "Make sustainable living commonplace" —
  marketing's job is normalisation, not preaching.

### Pattern 2 — Customer-anchored

The vision is anchored to a specific buyer reality — a segment, a
JTBD, a friction with their current alternative. Marketing's job is
to make the buyer's preferred future irresistible.

- **When to use:** the company serves a clear segment with a
  distinct pain (most B2B SaaS, most marketplaces).
- **Risk:** can feel small; needs a believable expansion story.
- **Example:** Airbnb's vision is anchored to the traveller's desire
  for authentic local experience over hotel sameness.

### Pattern 3 — Capability-anchored

The vision is anchored to a unique marketing capability the
organisation is uniquely positioned to build. The capability becomes
the moat.

- **When to use:** the company has (or can build) a defensible
  marketing capability that competitors structurally cannot copy
  (Apple's brand-as-product, Costco's membership-as-marketing,
  Salesforce's community-as-channel).
- **Risk:** requires honesty about whether the capability is really
  unique; many companies overestimate this.
- **Example:** "Make Salesforce's customer community the place every
  CRM buyer learns from peers first."

### Choosing between patterns

| Signal | Pattern |
|--------|---------|
| Company brand is rooted in values | Cause-anchored |
| Company sells to a clear segment with a clear pain | Customer-anchored |
| Company has a structural distribution or community moat | Capability-anchored |
| Company is a platform with multiple buyer types | Customer-anchored, with the dominant segment named first |
| Company is post-IPO and needs to defend valuation | Capability-anchored — the moat is the answer |

The strongest visions blend at least two patterns. Airbnb is
customer-anchored ("authentic local experiences") with a capability
implication (host marketplace). Unilever is cause-anchored with a
customer-behaviour bridge ("commonplace" = everyday buying).
