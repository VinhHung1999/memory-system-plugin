# Hypothesis Templates

Strong A/B tests start with strong hypotheses. Template:
**"I believe [change] will cause [metric] to [direction] by [magnitude] because [user-centric reason]."**

## CTR hypotheses

1. **Shorter headline** — "I believe 5-word headlines will increase CTR by 15% because mobile users scan in under 1 second."
2. **Personalization** — "I believe adding `{location}` to headlines will lift CTR by 20% because local relevance increases trust."
3. **Benefit vs feature** — "I believe benefit-led headlines ('Save 3 hours') will beat feature-led ('AI-powered scheduler') by 25% because outcomes are easier to evaluate."
4. **Number in headline** — "I believe headlines with numbers ('3 ways to…') will lift CTR by 18% because lists feel concrete and scannable."
5. **Question vs statement** — "I believe question headlines ('Struggling with X?') will increase CTR for pain-point-aware users by 15%."

## Conversion rate hypotheses

6. **Form field reduction** — "I believe reducing checkout form from 8 to 5 fields will lift conv rate by 30% because friction is the #1 abandonment reason."
7. **Guest checkout** — "I believe adding guest checkout option will lift conv rate by 20% because account-creation-forced users abandon 40% of the time."
8. **Trust signals** — "I believe adding trust badges near CTA will lift conv by 10% because price-sensitive users need assurance before committing."
9. **Single CTA vs multiple** — "I believe single primary CTA will beat two competing CTAs by 15% because decision fatigue reduces action."
10. **Social proof count** — "I believe showing 'X customers this week' will lift conv by 12% because recency proves active use."

## Revenue hypotheses

11. **Free shipping threshold** — "I believe raising free shipping threshold from $50 to $75 will lift AOV by 20% (less lift in conv but net revenue up)."
12. **Bundle offer** — "I believe bundling 2 products at 15% off will lift revenue per transaction by 30% because customers value savings more than single-item discount."
13. **Upsell at cart** — "I believe adding 'frequently bought together' at cart will lift AOV by 8% because intent is highest at cart."

## Email hypotheses

14. **Send time** — "I believe 10am Tuesday will beat 8am Tuesday by 10% open rate because decision-maker inboxes are less cluttered by mid-morning."
15. **Subject personalization** — "I believe adding `{first_name}` will lift open rate by 15% because personalized subject pops in crowded inbox."

## Anti-patterns (don't hypothesize these)

- **"Let's try X and see what happens"** — no hypothesis = no learning. Always predict direction + magnitude.
- **"X should be better"** — why? User-centric reasoning required.
- **"We think X is prettier"** — aesthetic preferences aren't hypotheses; test something measurable.

## Why hypothesis-first matters

Without a hypothesis, you can't know if you LEARNED anything — a "loss" teaches you just as much as a "win" if you predicted direction and the opposite happened. Tests without hypotheses just generate random winners and losers with no accumulating knowledge.

## MDE guidance

When writing hypothesis, pick realistic MDEs:
- **Headline tests:** 10-30% relative lift reasonable
- **CTA tests:** 5-15% relative lift
- **Form/checkout tests:** 10-50% possible if big simplification
- **Trust badge tests:** 2-10% relative lift
- **Copy tone tests:** 2-8% relative lift
- **Color/button tests:** 1-5% relative lift (usually not worth testing)

Don't try to detect <3% lifts unless you have massive traffic — sample size gets prohibitive.
